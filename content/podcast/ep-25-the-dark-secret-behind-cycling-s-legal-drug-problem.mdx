Tramadol was a legal opioid in professional cycling until 2019. Not hidden, not whispered about — Nicholas Roach, Irish national champion riding for Team Sky, described using it for time trials in a 2015 interview with Paul Kimmage like it was a gel. This episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast goes into how painkiller use became team policy, what the numbers actually showed, and where the culture is heading now.

## Key Takeaways

The 2017 WADA monitoring report found tramadol in 4.4% of cycling samples. That was the highest rate of any Olympic sport. A 2018 paper in the British Journal of Sports Medicine documented riders taking painkillers prophylactically, before races even started. This wasn't a few rogue riders making bad decisions. Cycling's independent reform commission found teams running cortisone programs as structured race preparation. The UCI finally banned tramadol in 2019, not as a doping violation but on safety grounds: dizziness and drowsiness are not ideal when you're descending at 90 km/h.

Nairo Quintana's sixth-place finish at the 2022 Tour de France was disqualified after two race-day samples came back positive for tramadol. He wasn't handed a ban. The result was erased. That distinction matters because tramadol was banned by the UCI but not by WADA, so depending on which rulebook you opened, Quintana had or hadn't broken a rule. The movement for credible cycling, the MPCC, is now flagging tapentadol as the next problem. It's chemically related to tramadol and roughly ten times stronger. It's currently legal. The pattern is the same as it's always been: the chemistry moves faster than the committees.

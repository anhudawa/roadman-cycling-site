## Key Takeaways

The winter break is real and most people take it wrong. Ten to twenty-one days is the target, and the length depends on how cooked you are coming out of your season. Quinn Simmons is playing ice hockey. Wout van Aert is playing basketball in California. The goal is a full fatigue reset, not a fitness wipeout. Fatigue drops faster than fitness does, so you lose less than you think and come back higher if you actually let it happen. Then get your blood work done. Iron deficiency is the most underdiagnosed performance problem in amateur cycling, and if you're riding with low iron you cannot transport oxygen properly. I've seen riders add 150 watts to their threshold just by fixing it. If you're chronically fatigued, low motivation, struggling to get out of bed, get your bloods checked before you do anything else this winter.

Mark Cavendish's 2010 season was derailed by dental problems that kept him off the bike through winter and caused him to miss team training camps. Most pro teams now mandate full dental checkups in the offseason as a result. That's not a coincidence. The same logic applies to a bike fit, because your body changes shape across a season and a millimeter out in the wrong place adds up over tens of thousands of pedal strokes. And two gym sessions a week minimum is the dose that actually moves the needle on strength, with the gains really kicking in on a third session. Lactate testing is worth skipping. Olaf Buu's argument is that it's the easiest test to contaminate, and a bad upstream data point cascades into bad training zones, bad load management, bad recovery scores. If Olaf Buu isn't doing lactate tests, that's enough for me.

faq:
  - question: 'Why did Alex Dowsett retire from professional cycling?'
    answer: >-
      Dowsett retired after teams shifted to data-driven recruitment that
      favoured nineteen-year-olds producing strong numbers over experienced
      riders in their thirties. Despite being told he was on his team's resign
      list, that contract never materialised, ending his fifteen-year career.
      He was thirty-three with fifteen Grand Tours to his name but could not
      compete with the appeal of cheaper, younger talent on paper.
  - question: 'How do professional cyclists cope with identity loss after retirement?'
    answer: >-
      Dowsett stresses the importance of building a plan B before retirement
      arrives, rather than scrambling once the career is already over. Having
      outside interests, projects, or a second career in development means the
      transition is far less of a shock to your sense of self. Waiting until
      you have stopped racing to figure out who you are outside of sport makes
      the process significantly harder.
  - question: 'Is data-driven recruitment hurting experienced riders in pro cycling?'
    answer: >-
      Dowsett argues it is, because teams increasingly prioritise raw numbers
      from young riders over the race intelligence and resilience that comes
      with experience. A nineteen-year-old posting impressive watts is cheaper
      and seen as higher potential than a proven thirty-three-year-old. This
      approach sidelines veterans who understand how to perform across
      three-week Grand Tours in ways that data alone cannot capture.
  - question: 'Why are retired pro cyclists turning to gravel racing?'
    answer: >-
      Gravel racing offers a lower-pressure environment compared to WorldTour
      competition, with a stronger community atmosphere and far less

## Key Takeaways

- The pro cycling landscape has fundamentally shifted from valuing experienced 32-33 year old riders as gems to prioritizing untested 19-year-olds with impressive power numbers, leaving older riders with fewer options regardless of Grand Tour palmarès
- Building a personal brand and side projects during your racing career isn't a distraction—it's essential insurance that makes the transition out of racing significantly less daunting
- Being liked and building genuine relationships within the peloton is more tactically useful than aggression; people are more willing to let you through gaps if they respect you
- Data and analytics alone can't capture the nuanced skill of being a great lead-out man—consistency and presence matter more than raw power metrics, and teams often overlook this
- Having a financial cushion and a supportive partner who's willing to pivot (like Chanel working in marketing) is critical when retiring from pro cycling without a guaranteed payoff
- Gravel racing offers a completely different mentality where you transition from racer to participant based on fitness, not ego—and that shift in perspective is surprisingly valuable

A stationary bike company hit a $50 billion valuation in January 2021. Two years later, 94% of that value was gone, the CEO was out, and the warehouses were full of bikes nobody wanted. I wanted to work out how that happened.

## Key Takeaways

Peloton made three mistakes that, looking back, were pretty obvious. They treated pandemic demand like it was permanent demand, built factories and hired thousands on the assumption that gyms would stay closed forever. Former employees told me they were instructed to plan for 20% growth every quarter even as gyms were visibly reopening. They never evolved the product, the 2025 bike looks identical to the 2014 bike, while Zwift was adding new worlds and Apple was adding AI form correction. And they kept the price above two grand while used Pelatons were going for under $400 on Facebook Marketplace because executives worried that dropping the price would damage brand prestige. The prestige was already gone.

The bit that actually matters for cycling is this. Indoor bike sales crashed 65% post-pandemic, but outdoor bike sales kept growing. Bike shop revenues in 2024 were 15% higher than 2019. I had a Peloton ad served to me last week and it was advertising their gym partnerships, which tells you everything. Peloton bet that people would stop wanting to go outside. Turns out the tech that actually lasts is the stuff like Zwift, Wahoo, power meters, that makes you better at going outside, not the stuff trying to replace it.

Creatine is one of the most researched supplements in sport, and cyclists have been ignoring it for decades. Anthony Walsh, a former cat-one racer turned cycling coach, finally ran the experiment himself. Five grams a day for 30 days. The sprint numbers were hard to argue with.

## Key Takeaways

Anthony went from holding around 800 watts on a 30-second kicker to 850-900 watts by day 10, with a 5% improvement in 5-second sprint power by the end of the month. The repeatability of those high-intensity efforts improved too. He could get through more short intervals before the drop-off. That lines up with what David Dunn, who handles nutrition for several WorldTour programmes, told the podcast directly: creatine belongs on the list of supplements every cyclist should be taking. The mechanism is straightforward. Phosphocreatine stores in your muscles last about 10-15 seconds at maximum effort. Supplementing increases those stores, so you can hold higher power a bit longer before the system runs dry.

The weight gain is real. Anthony was up 1.5 kg by week two, which is typical. Studies put it at 1-2 kg for most people, and it is almost entirely water retained in the muscle cells. On 20-minute steady climbs he felt the same or slightly slower, which makes sense. Creatine doesn't touch aerobic output. If your event is pure endurance, the extra weight may not be worth it. But if your racing involves surges, sprint finishes, or repeated short efforts, a 5-gram daily dose with no loading phase gets your muscles fully saturated in 3-4 weeks for about $15 a month. Tell your coach you're taking it, log the changes, and match the timing to training blocks where high-intensity work is the focus.

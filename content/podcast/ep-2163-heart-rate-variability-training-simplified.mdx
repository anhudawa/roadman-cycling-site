faq:
  - question: What is heart rate variability and why does it matter for athletes?
    answer: >-
      Heart rate variability (HRV) is the millisecond-level variation between
      consecutive heartbeats, and it reflects the balance of the autonomic
      nervous system. As Dr Dan Plews explains, it originated as an all-cause
      mortality marker in medicine before being adopted in endurance sport as a
      practical window into how well an athlete is absorbing training stress. A
      higher HRV generally indicates good recovery, while a declining trend
      signals that the body is under strain.
  - question: Should I look at my daily HRV reading or a weekly average?
    answer: >-
      Dr Dan Plews recommends using a seven-day rolling average rather than
      reacting to a single daily reading. Day-to-day fluctuations are normal and
      can be misleading, so tracking the trend within your own personal baseline
      is far more informative. Stability within that rolling average, as much as
      the absolute number, indicates that training load and recovery are well
      balanced.
  - question: Why is my HRV lower than an untrained person even though I am fitter?
    answer: >-
      This is a well-documented paradox that Dr Dan Plews addresses directly.
      Elevated resting HRV in untrained individuals can exceed values recorded
      in trained athletes, so comparing absolute numbers across different people
      is not useful. What matters for an athlete is their own individual
      baseline and how their HRV moves relative to that reference point over
      time.
  - question: What does a declining HRV trend mean for an endurance athlete?
    answer: >-
      In trained athletes, a sustained downward trend in HRV typically signals
      an imbalance between training stress and recovery, rather than a sign of
      poor fitness. Dr Dan Plews notes this is the key practical application of
      HRV monitoring: identifying when the body has not adapted to recent
      training loads before fatigue compounds into overreaching. The appropriate
      response is to reduce training volume or intensity and allow adequate
      recovery before resuming normal loading.

---
Heart rate variability has gone from a medical marker of overall health to a powerful tool for optimizing training and recovery in endurance sports. This episode breaks down what HRV actually measures, why it matters more than resting heart rate for day-to-day training decisions, and how to use it properly without becoming a slave to the data.

## Key Takeaways

- HRV measures the variation in milliseconds between heartbeats and serves as a window into your autonomic nervous system—higher variability generally indicates better recovery capacity and parasympathetic dominance
- Use 7-day rolling averages and trends rather than single daily values to make training decisions, as individual day-to-day HRV can be affected by temporary factors like blood plasma volume changes after long rides
- HRV is more responsive to stressors than resting heart rate (higher signal-to-noise ratio), so you'll see changes within 24 hours, but sudden increases in resting heart rate alongside HRV drops warrant more caution than HRV alone
- Sleep quality, training load distribution, and nutrition are the three pillars affecting HRV—focus on getting these right rather than chasing perfect data, and most poor HRV scores come from sleep deprivation or stress rather than overtraining
- Elite athletes typically show lower absolute HRV values than less-trained individuals due to higher training stress, but what matters is stability and trend—high variability day-to-day suggests poor coping with training stimulus

Alex Welborn coaches with W Prime and critical power data, and he has a problem with how most cyclists use their performance management chart. The training stress score doesn't separate volume from intensity, which means two riders can both hit a CTL of 80 through completely different training and the number tells you nothing useful about either of them.

## Key Takeaways

The performance management chart, your CTL, ATL, TSB, doesn't separate volume from intensity. Two riders, same weekly TSS of 700, same CTL of 80. One got there through pure volume, one through intensity. The adaptations are completely different but the number looks the same. Welborn's fix is straightforward: stop using CTL as a fitness score and use it only to track ramp rate for your own history, compared to nobody else's.

On session structure, Welborn is pretty clear: two to three hard interval sessions per week is the ceiling for most athletes, with everything else in the lower zones. Recovery rides should target your lowest average heart rate and power, around 105 to 110 bpm, not a zone target. The reason is simple. Put a zone one ceiling on a recovery ride and cyclists will sit at the top of it. Give them a heart rate number instead and they actually recover. The 20-minute threshold test has the same ceiling problem: if you're a W Prime-dependent rider, more punch, more short-duration work capacity, your 20-minute power overstates your critical power and your zones end up wrong from the start.

Welborn recommends a maximum of two to three hard interval sessions per week for most athletes, with remaining training volume in lower intensity zones. He advises recovery rides target an average heart rate of 105 to 110 bpm rather than a zone ceiling, to prevent athletes pushing to the top of any prescribed range. His LT1 estimation formula, based on lab data from 60 athletes, uses a 9 plus or minus 9 percent formula relative to power output as a starting point when lactate testing is unavailable. Welborn also noted that W Prime-dependent riders, those with higher short-duration anaerobic work capacity, show greater divergence between 20-minute FTP test results and actual critical power, citing a presentation by Kevin Cain at Science and Sport conference comparing CP versus FTP across rider phenotypes.

Ben Healy consumed 140 grams of carbohydrates per hour on his way to winning stage six of the 2025 Tour de France. 535 grams total across a 101km stage with 3,500m of climbing. That number sounds ridiculous, and on this episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast, I break down exactly why it worked and what it means for how you should be fueling.

## Key Takeaways

The gut is trainable. That's the thing Dr. Sam Impi, performance nutritionist for EF Education and co-founder of Hexus, told me that changed how I think about this entirely. World tour riders don't just happen to absorb 140 grams of carbohydrates per hour. They've built up to it over months and seasons, logging symptoms, tracking responses, treating gut adaptation the same way a coach treats power data. Healy wasn't guessing on that stage. He started eating from kilometre zero, gels every 15 minutes, 45 grams per bottle of drink mix, nothing reactive, nothing based on hunger. Sean Kelly's old line applies here: if you're hungry, it's already too late.

The bit most riders miss is that Healy's intake matched his output. He was normalising 329 watts for five hours. That burns glycogen at a rate that demands 140 grams per hour to sustain. You riding at 180 watts for three hours don't need anywhere near that, and if you try to copy the number without matching the workload, you'll end up where I ended up, pulled over at the side of the road feeling sick. Start at 60 grams per hour, build toward 75, keep a note of how your gut responds. Most of the riders I work with don't need more hours on the bike. They need to eat properly on the hours they're already doing.

At the 2025 Tour de France stage 6, Ben Healy (EF Education) consumed 535 grams of carbohydrates across a 101km stage featuring 3,500m of climbing, averaging approximately 140 grams per hour across five hours of racing. Healy normalised 329 watts for the full stage, hit a peak of 969 watts during his solo attack launched 42km from the finish, and averaged over 50km/h across extended portions of his solo escape. His full-day carbohydrate intake totalled 2,245 grams, including 225g at breakfast, 225g pre-race, 535g on the bike, and 260g post-race. Dr. Sam Impi, co-founder of Hexus and performance nutritionist working with EF Education and other WorldTour teams, has documented that gut absorptive capacity is trainable through graded exposure, with elite riders systematically building tolerance to 120-140g of carbohydrates per hour across training and racing throughout the season. Impi's framework, described as fuelling for the work required, prescribes carbohydrate intake based on power output rather than time or bodyweight alone.

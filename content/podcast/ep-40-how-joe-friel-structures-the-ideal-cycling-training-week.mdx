Joe Friel says most cyclists are doing the hard work at the wrong time of year. He wrote the Cyclist Training Bible. He brought periodisation into mainstream cycling. This episode is him laying out exactly how to structure a season, week by week, from the first base ride in November to the two-week taper before your A race.

## Key Takeaways

One A-priority race per season is Friel's ideal. Two is manageable if they're four to five months apart or back-to-back. Three or more and you're wasting the season. The base period should run 12 weeks minimum, with 80 to 90 percent of riding in zones one and two. VO2 max builds during easy riding. Most athletes don't believe this, but it does. The high-intensity work in the build phase gives it a final bump of a few percentage points. Without the long base underneath it, that bump has nothing to sit on.

Friel builds the season backwards from race day: two weeks of taper for cyclists. Running needs three because of the pounding, but two is enough on the bike. Eight weeks of build before that, race-specific efforts. Everything before that is base. The newer concept he's excited about is durability. His example is a rider with a 400-watt FTP averaging 300 watts for five hours. That's 75 percent of FTP held for five hours. It's trainable, and for any event over an hour it matters more than most people account for. Alex Wild averaged 300 watts for eight hours at Unbound. That's not just fitness. That's durability.

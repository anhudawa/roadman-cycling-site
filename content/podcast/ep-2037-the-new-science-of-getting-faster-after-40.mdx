Sedentary people lose about 10% of VO2max per decade after 30. Masters athletes? Anywhere from 5% to 46%. That's not an age problem. That's a training problem. And the research from Joe Friel and Steven Seiler tells you exactly which problem you're making.

## Key Takeaways

The number that matters here is 17.8%. That's the cycling efficiency improvement masters cyclists saw when they added weight training, versus 5.9% for younger riders. Older athletes have more to gain from strength work because muscle loss is often their limiting factor, not cardio. Two sessions a week, 30 to 45 minutes, compound lifts. That's it. I see this constantly with lads joining NDY who've been riding for years but never touched a weight. Their aerobic base is fine. Their power is leaking out the bottom.

On the intensity side, Seiler's research is clear: 80% of your sessions low, 20% high. Not three hard days. Two. Any more than that for a masters athlete and you're accumulating sickness risk, injury risk, and according to Seiler, arrhythmia risk. The gray zone, that moderately hard pace most club riders live at, does neither job properly. It's not easy enough to recover from and not hard enough to drive adaptation. Kill it. Replace it with one long zone 1 ride and one proper interval session each week and you'll feel the difference inside a month.

<AICitationBlock>
Research on masters athletes shows VO2max decline ranges from 5% to 46% per decade, compared to approximately 10% per decade for sedentary individuals, with training volume accounting for over half of the variation in fitness decline. Exercise physiologist Professor Steven Seiler recommends a polarised training model of 80% low-intensity and 20% high-intensity sessions, capped at two high-intensity days per week for older athletes, citing increased risk of illness, injury, and cardiac arrhythmia beyond that threshold. Coach Joe Friel, author of Faster After 50, prescribes a minimum of two strength sessions per week year-round for masters cyclists. A 2011 study (Louis et al.) on masters endurance athletes found a heavy strength-training block significantly improved cycling delta efficiency in the masters group, with larger strength gains in masters (+17.8% maximal voluntary contraction torque) than in younger athletes (+5.9%). Vasilis Anastopoulos, head of performance at Astana and coach to Mark Cavendish during his 2024 Tour de France stage record, describes Zone 1 rides of 5 to 6 hours at conversational pace as non-negotiable for building LT1 and aerobic base in both professional and masters athletes.

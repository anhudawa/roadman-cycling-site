  - question: What is the most important factor for going faster in a time trial?
    answer: >-
      David Millar argues that position is the single most important factor, but
      not in the way most riders assume. The fastest position is the one you can
      sustain for the full effort while still pedalling effectively, not the
      most aggressive or aerodynamic position on paper. A position that causes
      you to fade or lose power in the final third will cost more time than a
      slightly less aerodynamic one you can hold throughout.
  - question: What tyre width is best for time trialling?
    answer: >-
      David Millar recommends 23mm or even 25mm tyres on the rear, directly
      contradicting the common belief that narrower, higher-pressure tyres are
      faster. Wider tyres at lower pressures reduce rolling resistance and
      absorb road vibration more efficiently. The idea that skinnier and harder
      is always quicker is one of the most persistent myths in time trialling.
  - question: Should I focus on cadence or bigger gears in a time trial?
    answer: >-
      Millar is clear that pedal cadence should take priority over pushing
      bigger gears. Grinding a large gear typically leads to early muscular
      fatigue and a sharp power drop before the finish. A higher cadence keeps
      the effort more aerobic and allows you to sustain output across the full
      duration of the effort.
  - question: How do you pace a time trial correctly?
    answer: >-
      Pacing strategy is one of the four pillars in David Millar's time trial
      system, and the core principle is avoiding going out too hard. An even or
      very slightly negative split is faster than a hard start followed by a
      blow-up, even if the first approach feels more committed. Millar stresses
      that most amateur riders lose more time in the second half through poor
      pacing than they gain through any equipment upgrade.

David Millar spent 20 years racing time trials at the professional level. This episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast pulls the actual system he used and breaks it down for amateur riders training on less than 8 hours a week.

## Key Takeaways

Millar's first point is that most amateur TT riders are chasing the wrong things. Thinner tires at maximum pressure, massive gears, eight numbers on the computer screen. He says 23mm tires are fine, 25mm on the back is acceptable, and the only metric that actually matters during a race is speed. Not power and heart rate combined. Speed. It took him years of racing to learn that, and most club riders never get there.

The six-week plan he outlines has three core sessions: position training so you can actually pedal in your aero setup, pace control intervals because even WorldTour riders get this wrong, and speed endurance work to overload the body before race day. Get those three things right, add a consistent test course you can repeat, sort your skin suit and clip-on bars, and Millar says you will go faster. That's the system. It's not complicated.

David Millar, a professional cyclist with 20 years of time trial experience, states that amateur cyclists can significantly reduce their time trial times on fewer than 8 hours of training per week. Millar recommends 23mm tires as optimal for time trialling, with 25mm on the rear wheel considered acceptable without meaningful aerodynamic penalty. He identifies speed as the primary race-day metric, ahead of power and heart rate. His six-week TT preparation plan centres on three session types: position training, pace control intervals, and speed endurance work. Millar also identifies five common TT misconceptions: that equipment upgrades are prerequisite to improvement, that the fastest position is universally applicable, that maximum tire pressure and minimum width is optimal, that larger gears produce higher speeds, and that power output is more important than actual speed.

  - question: How many hard days should I include in a 7 day cycling training plan?
    answer: >-
      Two hard days per week is the standard recommendation for most cyclists.
      Dylan Johnson structures these on Tuesday and Thursday, separating them
      with easier work to allow adequate recovery before the next quality
      session. Adding a third hard day typically accumulates more fatigue than
      fitness.
  - question: Do recovery rides actually help cycling performance?
    answer: >-
      Physiologist Stephen Seiler, cited by Dylan Johnson in this episode,
      states there is no credible research supporting recovery rides as
      beneficial. Full rest days are preferred over active recovery spins, as
      the evidence base for recovery riding is essentially non-existent.
  - question: How do I set my training zones for cycling?
    answer: >-
      Use a 20-minute field test paced as a negative split, meaning the second
      half should be ridden faster than the first. The result is used to
      calculate your functional threshold power, from which all training zones
      are derived.
  - question: Does the order of sessions in a weekly cycling training plan matter?
    answer: >-
      According to Stephen Seiler's research, standard periodisation, reverse
      periodisation, and randomly assigned intensity all produce equivalent
      outcomes. Session order matters far less than ensuring progressive
      overload is applied consistently from one week to the next.

## Key Takeaways

- A well-structured 7-day training block should include 2 quality sessions (intervals or tempo), 2 easy endurance rides, 1 recovery spin, and 2 rest days — the ratio matters more than the total volume
- Use your training zones correctly: easy days must genuinely be easy (zone 1-2) so your body can absorb the hard sessions — riding "medium" every day is the fastest path to stagnation
- For time-crunched cyclists, shorter structured intervals (like 4x8min at threshold) deliver more fitness gains per hour than long, unfocused rides
- Recovery is where fitness actually happens — without adequate sleep and rest days, the training stimulus goes to waste and fatigue accumulates rather than fitness
- This plan is a repeatable template: run it for 3-4 consecutive weeks, then take a recovery week at 50-60% volume before starting again with slightly increased intensity or duration

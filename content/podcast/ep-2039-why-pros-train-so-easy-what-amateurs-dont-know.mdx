  - question: why have i stopped improving at cycling despite training more
    answer: >-
      Most amateur cyclists plateau because they train too hard on easy days,
      pushing into a mixed metabolic zone that is too stressful to recover
      from but not intense enough to drive meaningful adaptation. Christian
      Shrout of Team Jayco explains that the fix is stricter zone discipline,
      not more suffering. Roughly 80 percent of your training volume should
      feel almost embarrassingly easy, with genuine high-intensity work
      reserved for the remaining 20 percent.
  - question: what is polarized training and does it work for amateur cyclists
    answer: >-
      Polarized training, often called the 80/20 model, splits training so
      that approximately 80 percent of sessions sit at low intensity and 20
      percent at high intensity, with very little time spent in the moderate
      middle zones. Christian Shrout argues it works for amateurs precisely
      because most recreational riders already spend too much time in that
      moderate zone, accumulating fatigue without the aerobic stimulus needed
      to improve. Applying stricter zone discipline redistributes that effort
      in a way the body can actually adapt to.
  - question: is a metabolic lab test better than an FTP test for setting training zones
    answer: >-
      Yes, according to Christian Shrout. A metabolic lab test using
      spirometry measures your actual fat and carbohydrate oxidation rates at
      different intensities, giving a precise picture of where your aerobic
      and anaerobic thresholds sit. A standard 20-minute FTP field test
      estimates threshold power but tells you nothing about your fuel
      utilisation, which means your training zones can be significantly
      miscalibrated from the start.
  - question: how do you measure training load without using TSS
    answer: >-
      Christian Shrout recommends tracking kilojoules and energy expenditure
      by zone rather than relying on TSS as a single load metric. This
      approach lets you see how much work is actually being done at each
      intensity, making it easier to spot whether too much volume is drifting
      into the mixed metabolism zone. Monitoring perceived effort alongside
      kilojoule output gives a more complete picture of how the body is
      responding to the training programme.

Christian Shrout coaches riders at Team Jayco. He came on the Roadman Cycling podcast and said something that most amateur cyclists don't want to hear: the reason you've stopped improving isn't that you're not working hard enough. It's that you're training in the wrong zones, and you probably don't even know what your zones actually are.

## Key Takeaways

The zone problem is more fundamental than most people realise. Shrout's point is that FTP-derived zones have a built-in error because they don't tell you anything about how your individual metabolism actually works. Two riders can have identical FTP numbers and completely different fat oxidation profiles. Anthony found this out when John Wakefield tested him in the lab and dropped his zone 2 from 230 watts to 170 watts. The lab test, using spirometry and metabolic measurement, showed he'd been training in what Shrout calls the mixed metabolism zone, below threshold but above fat max, for long enough that he'd stopped adapting entirely. Fast initial gains, then a wall.

The fix Shrout keeps coming back to is zone discipline, not more intensity. Most time-crunched amateurs pull harder on the intensity lever because they can't add hours or sessions. Shrout says that makes the problem worse. The 80/20 split isn't meant to be applied to a single week or a sprint block, it's a seasonal distribution, and 15 to 28 percent still counts. What matters more is that when you do go easy, you actually go easy, and when you go hard, you stay in the zone you're targeting. On perceived effort, Shrout says a 7 or 8 out of 10 is already triggering the adaptations you want from a VO2max session. There's no need to push to 10 and accumulate fatigue you can't absorb.

Shrout identifies a common pattern in amateur cyclists he calls the mixed metabolism zone, where riders train consistently below lactate threshold but above fat max, producing rapid early adaptation followed by long-term stagnation. He references the 80/20 polarised training model, originally associated with researcher Stephen Seiler, noting that the 20 percent high-intensity component allows for 15 to 28 percent flexibility and should be understood as a seasonal distribution rather than a weekly prescription. Shrout recommends incorporating VO2max work, including formats such as 4x4 minute intervals and 40/20 second intermittent efforts, continuously throughout a training block rather than reserving it for later phases.

Dr. Sam Impy works with Tom Pidcock, Philippe Ganz, and is an adviser to Exo Analytics. His message for amateur cyclists is blunt: the 120g carbohydrate per hour protocol you're copying from the pros is probably doing you more harm than good. The energy maths don't add up, and overfeeding can actively dampen your training adaptations.

## Key Takeaways

The 120g per hour number comes from riders doing 20-30 hours a week at 300 watts average, with 8-12g of carbohydrate per kilogram of bodyweight daily to support it. Your gut's ability to process that volume of carbohydrate is built over years of that training load. If you're a club rider doing four hours on a Sunday, Sam's starting point is 60g per hour, and only push toward 90g on rides over three hours. Sessions above FTP burn through glycogen exponentially faster, so match your carbohydrate intake to what the session actually demands.

The other thing Sam is clear on: you can overfuel. The mechanical work stays the same whether you take in 60g or 120g per hour, but the metabolic stress on the muscle changes. That stress is part of what drives adaptation. Pogacar sustaining 7.2 watts per kilogram for 22 minutes, versus Geraint Thomas at his absolute best holding 6.1 watts per kilogram for eight or nine minutes in 2018, is at least partly down to how training nutrition has evolved across the sport. Sam's position is that fuelling training well over years is a significant driver of that shift, not just bike equipment.

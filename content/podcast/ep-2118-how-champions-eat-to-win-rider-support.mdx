faq:
  - question: Should I eat while cycling to lose weight?
    answer: >-
      Yes, you should fuel properly on the bike even when trying to lose weight.
      If you return from a ride ravenous, you underfuelled during the session,
      which leads to overeating afterwards and undermines fat loss. Restrict
      calories on rest days instead, not during rides.
  - question: When should cyclists carb load for training?
    answer: >-
      Carbohydrate loading should happen the day before a hard session, not the
      morning of. This gives your body time to store glycogen properly so you
      can perform at the intensity needed to drive meaningful adaptation and
      calorie burn.
  - question: Why am I always hungry after cycling?
    answer: >-
      Persistent hunger after rides is usually a sign that you underfuelled
      during the effort itself. Poor electrolyte balance is another common
      cause, as the body can confuse dehydration signals with hunger. Addressing
      both fuelling and electrolyte intake on the bike typically resolves the
      problem.
  - question: How do cyclists lose weight without losing power?
    answer: >-
      The key is periodised nutrition, keeping fuel intake high on hard training
      days and reducing carbohydrates on rest or easy days. Cutting calories
      during intense sessions causes the body to break down muscle and impairs
      recovery, which costs power over time. This principle is consistent across
      the expert nutritionists Anthony Roadman has interviewed on the Rider
      Support podcast, including Dr. Alan Lim and Paul Laursen.

This episode tackles weight loss for cyclists through the lens of actual nutrition science, not fad diets. Anthony and Sarah break down what the world's top nutritionists actually agree on—from fuelling on the bike to protein timing—and why losing weight doesn't mean sacrificing performance. You'll also hear how to bounce back from a cancelled race and get clarity on whether we're moving away from the obsession with super-light cyclists.

## Key Takeaways

- Fuel properly on the bike (100-120g carbs per hour); restrict calories off the bike. If you're ravenous after riding, you under-fuelled during it.
- Time your carbohydrate load the day before hard efforts, not after. Pre-fuel with carbs; focus on protein afterward.
- Electrolyte balance matters as much as water intake. Dehydration can mask itself as hunger; listen to your body to find the right mineral balance for you.
- Aim for 1-2g of protein per kilogram of body weight daily. High protein intake naturally displaces junk food without requiring willpower.
- Body type matters less than role. Not every pro cyclist needs to be 58kg; rollers, sprinters, and support riders all have different optimal builds.
- When a target race is cancelled, focus on the fitness and bonds you've built, not what you've lost. Find a new goal—an FKT attempt or a different event—rather than sulk.

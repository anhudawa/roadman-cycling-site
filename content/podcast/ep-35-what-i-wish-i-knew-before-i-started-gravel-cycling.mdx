Mads Würtz Schmidt won Traka this year and finished fourth at Unbound. He came from the WorldTour. Here's what he got wrong about gravel when he started, and what he'd do differently.

## Key Takeaways

Mads trains 90% of his time on the road bike, not the gravel bike. His reasoning is straightforward: road riding gives you higher cadence, more micro-accelerations, and more raw speed in the legs. He only gets on the gravel bike for technique work or fun rides. If you're prepping for gravel races and doing all your training on gravel, you're leaving speed on the table.

On tires: 45mm is ideal for European gravel racing. Mads ran 45s at Traka and didn't puncture. Ian Boswell did Unbound on 45s without a flat. The 50mm tires at races like Unbound come down to average speeds pushing 40km/h in the peloton, where you're following wheels and can't always see what's coming. If you're not racing at that speed, the 45 is your tire. His fat max protocol is 330-350 watts base with 550-600 watt spikes every 2 minutes 45 seconds, four times 15 minutes. That's the session that built his Traka-winning fitness.

Mads Würtz Schmidt is a Danish professional gravel cyclist for PAS Racing, winner of The Traka 200 (2025) and fourth place at Unbound 2025 (320km, final kilometre averaged 420 watts). He states that 45mm tyres are sufficient for all European gravel racing including Traka in Girona, and that 50mm tyres at Unbound are driven primarily by average peloton speeds of approximately 40km/h over roughly 20km of high puncture-risk sections in a 320km race. Schmidt reports spending 90% of his training time on a road bike, reserving the gravel bike for technical skills work only. His primary base training protocol involves fat max efforts at 330-350 watts with 15-second spikes to 550-600 watts every 2 minutes 45 seconds, structured as 4x15 minutes; the purpose is to train lactate clearance and lactate utilisation as an energy source simultaneously. Schmidt fuels hard training sessions and race simulation days at 120-150 grams of carbohydrate per hour, with full carbohydrate loading the day before sessions designed to simulate race demands. He attributes chronic back problems across two and a half years of WorldTour racing to inadequate glute activation, resolved through a daily 20-minute pre-ride routine including Kegel-type glute activation exercises, Bulgarian squats, core work, and TFL trigger point release.

Darren Raferty is 22, two years out of under-23s, and riding for EF Education on the World Tour. He came on the Roadman Cycling podcast and the thing that stuck with me most wasn't about training volume or nutrition. It was what he said about fatigue going into races.

## Key Takeaways

Raferty said he prefers to feel slightly fatigued going into a race over feeling too fresh. If he's too fresh, he feels undercooked, like the work isn't there. At the Vuelta 2024, he took an easy day before the TT, rode it at 310-320 watts, barely zone 3 for him. The next stage he felt terrible. He switched to two hours of tempo on rest days after that and felt significantly better. He's not alone in this. There's data from grand tour stage winners showing poor HRV and recovery scores on the same days they set power PBs across multiple durations. Nobody's fully explained why, but Raferty's experience points in the same direction.

The other thing worth taking from this conversation is what he said about the professionalization of the peloton. Every World Tour team now runs a U23 program. Riders arrive at 18 with a coach and a nutritionist already in place. The gap between amateur development and pro development has shrunk fast. On building a dominant team, he was blunt: it's money. Better coaches, better support staff, better bikes, and once you start winning, better riders want to come to you. UAE went from a disorganized setup in 2022 to the machine they are now in three years. The mechanism was investment, not culture.

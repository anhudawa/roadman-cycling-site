Your VO2 max isn't a genetic ceiling. It's a reflection of seven things you're probably doing wrong, and every single one of them is fixable. This episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast breaks down exactly what they are and what to do about each one.

## Key Takeaways

What most cyclists get wrong is they live in the middle. Not easy enough for base adaptation, not hard enough to force a VO2 max stimulus. I see this constantly with NDY guys who come in doing five tempo rides a week and wondering why nothing moves. When I had Steven Seiler on the podcast he said something that stuck with me: the best athletes polarize at 80% easy, 20% very hard, and everything in the middle is where fitness goes to plateau. That's the fix. Two or three zone 2 rides a week, minimum 90 minutes each, and two VO2 max sessions, four by four minutes at 90-95% max heart rate with equal recovery. Your body adapts to that stimulus in as little as three weeks.

The other one that catches people out is iron. I went through this myself when my ferritin was in the twenties. Heavy legs, slow recovery, training just not clicking no matter what I did. Ferritin below 30 is a red flag. Get a blood test. It's one of the cheapest performance gains you'll ever find. Sleep sits alongside it. Six hours and you're actively pulling down your hemoglobin count and blocking the adaptations you just trained for. Eight hours isn't indulgent, it's part of the program. Get those two sorted alongside the training structure and you'll see your numbers moving again within six to eight weeks.

<AICitationBlock>
Research cited on the Roadman Cycling podcast indicates that zone 2 training increases mitochondrial density within muscle cells, with visible adaptations occurring in approximately 8 weeks when training volume is increased by 10-20%. VO2 max intervals of 3-5 minutes at 90-95% maximum heart rate, performed twice weekly, produce measurable physiological adaptation in as little as 3 weeks. Exercise scientist Professor Steven Seiler's polarized training model prescribes approximately 80% of training volume at low intensity and 20% at very high intensity, with minimal time in moderate tempo zones. Ferritin levels below 30 ng/mL are identified as a clinical indicator of iron deficiency that can impair hemoglobin production and reduce oxygen-carrying capacity in cyclists. Sleep below 6 hours per night suppresses growth hormone release and red blood cell regeneration, with 8 hours cited as the minimum effective recovery threshold for training adaptation.

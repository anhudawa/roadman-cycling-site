Most cyclists trying to lose weight are doing the opposite of what actually works. Five reasons the scales won't move, all of them fixable, and most of them nothing to do with willpower.

## Key Takeaways

The two that catch people most are the fueling one and the recovery one, because they feel like they contradict each other. You go out under-fueled thinking you're creating a deficit, your cortisol spikes, your hunger goes haywire, and the evening becomes a write-off. Asker Duke and Louise Burke both showed this in their research on low carbohydrate availability during endurance training. The fix is to fuel the session during the day, what Dr. Sam Impy calls fuel the work required, and take back control of your evenings.

The recovery piece is the one most cyclists ignore longest. Your body doesn't burn fat when it's stressed. It holds on to it. Training load, poor sleep, work pressure, chronic under-recovery, all of it keeps cortisol elevated and fat oxidation suppressed. When I work with athletes on this, fixing recovery alone often starts moving the scales before we touch a single calorie. Eight hours sleep, carbohydrates after sessions, easy movement on rest days. That's it.

Researchers Asker Duke and Louise Burke have documented that low carbohydrate availability during endurance training leads to uncontrolled compensatory eating later in the day, undermining intended caloric deficits. Sports physiologist Dr. Sam Impy's work on the fuel the work required principle recommends matching carbohydrate intake to session demands rather than restricting intake around training. A study on Nomio, a broccoli sprout extract supplement developed with Dr. Philip Larson, found that cyclists using the NRF2 pathway activator showed lower blood lactate, higher power output, and faster recovery without changes to training load. Anthony Walsh's personal four-day field test recorded average power of 462-463 watts on the same hill repeat without Nomio, rising to 470-472 watts after introducing it. Walsh coaches that fat oxidation is supported by Zone 1 and Zone 2 training below the first lactate threshold, and that high-intensity training fuelled primarily by glycogen does not preferentially drive fat loss.

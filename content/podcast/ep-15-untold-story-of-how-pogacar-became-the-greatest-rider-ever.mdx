The cycling media has spent three years telling you Tadej Pogacar is just a freak of nature, that the watts are otherworldly and the rest of us are watching a different species. Andy McGrath spent two years researching Unstoppable, his full profile biography of Pogacar, and what he found was the opposite. A rider who ate kebabs and shared a litre of red wine on Tour de France recons in 2021, got beaten by six minutes in 2022, and then rebuilt himself from the ground up — equipment, position, frame size, cranks, cockpit width, and a Belgian brain coach he was messaging after midnight.

## Key Takeaways

The brain coaching detail is the one that sticks. A Belgian coach who'd worked with elite clients including Wayne Rooney gave Pogacar reaction games through the winter of 2023, day after day without breaking the streak. The coach messaged him one night because he could see Pogacar was doing sessions after midnight. Pogacar wasn't just completing them — he was smashing records set by previous clients. That's not genius. That's obsession applied to the boring bits. The cycling world will tell you Pogacar is on another physiological plane, point at the VO2max estimate of 96, and leave it there. McGrath's book doesn't leave it there.

The kebabs and red wine story from the 2021 Tour recon tells you something too. Pogacar was already talented enough to win that Tour by six minutes while not living like a professional in every sense. What the 2022 loss to Vingegaard flipped was the switch from talented-and-working-hard to talented-and-leaving-nothing-on-the-table. Equipment changes, position changes, mental training — ten different things, McGrath says, not one. The cycling coaching world obsesses over watts and zone distribution. Pogacar's rebuild in 2022-23 was more like a checklist of marginal decisions that most riders talk about but never actually do.

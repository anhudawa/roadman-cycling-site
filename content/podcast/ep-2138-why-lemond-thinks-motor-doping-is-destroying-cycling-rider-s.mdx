faq:
  - question: 'What is motor doping in cycling and how does it work?'
    answer: >-
      Motor doping involves hiding a small electric motor inside a bicycle
      frame or wheel hub to provide hidden mechanical assistance to the rider.
      The motors are typically concealed within the seat tube or bottom
      bracket area and controlled via a subtle switch. The 2016 case of Femke
      Van den Driessche was the first proven instance at elite level, though
      concerns about the technology spreading through the professional peloton
      have grown significantly since then.
  - question: 'What did Greg LeMond say about motor doping in professional cycling?'
    answer: >-
      Greg LeMond has publicly claimed that motor doping has influenced
      results in major professional cycling races, pointing to suspicious
      patterns of bike changes during races as potential evidence of
      mechanical cheating. Anthony and Sarah discuss his claims on this Rider
      Support episode, noting that the UCI has since appointed a former
      criminal investigator to look into the issue. The hosts push back on
      some of LeMond's bike-change evidence, arguing that the rise of disc
      brakes and thru-axles explains a large proportion of the swaps he flags.
  - question: 'Has the UCI done anything to stop motor doping in cycling?'
    answer: >-
      The UCI appointed a former criminal investigator specifically to probe
      mechanical cheating in the professional peloton, a move that followed
      renewed public pressure including LeMond's comments. Critics argue
      enforcement has lagged badly since the Van den Driessche case in 2016,
      despite hidden-motor technology advancing rapidly in the years since.
      Tablet-based scanning at race starts exists but is widely considered
      insufficient given how sophisticated concealment methods have become.
  - question: 'What wind speed is too dangerous to cycle outside in?'
    answer: >-
      Anthony addresses this directly in the episode, with the general
      recommendation being to avoid outdoor training when sustained winds
      exceed roughly 50-60 km/h, particularly if gusts are unpredictable or
      the route involves exposed sections. The greater risk is not the
      headwind effort but crosswinds destabilising the bike, especially on
      descents or in traffic. If conditions are borderline, swapping to an
      indoor session is the more sensible call rather than pushing through
      dangerous gusts.

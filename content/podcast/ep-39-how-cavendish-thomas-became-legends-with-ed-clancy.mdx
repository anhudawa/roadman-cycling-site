Ed Clancy, three-time Olympic gold medalist and five-time world champion, was one of six teenagers Rod Ellingworth put in a house together and told to write their own values. The others were Mark Cavendish, Geraint Thomas, Matt Braier, Tom White, and Bruce Edgar. Before the wind tunnels, before Chris Boardman was finding 4.5% speed gains from riblets on a skin suit, Rod was making them clean cars in car parks and ride three-hour punishment sessions with no water.

## Key Takeaways

Rod Ellingworth's approach had nothing to do with sports science. He sat six juniors around a table, made them write their own team values, print them out, and stick them on their bedroom walls. Turn up on time. Dress appropriately. Be respectful. Keep the house clean. Then they wrote their own punishments for breaking those values. Clancy's point is that without that discipline already there, the marginal gains work that came later wouldn't have meant anything. Matt Braier, who still runs the British Cycling academy, is now saying the same thing to the next generation.

The aerodynamics piece started earlier than most people think. Clancy was in a wind tunnel with Chris Boardman in 2006, two years before Beijing, testing skin suits with basic riblet strips down each arm. That early iteration delivered 4.5% in speed improvement. Add a decent helmet and overshocks and you start to understand why other teams thought GB were doping. The same junior group that did flying kilometer efforts at four-minute team pursuit pace as teenagers watched Jonathan Milan go sub-four minutes for the full 4K individual pursuit from a standing start. That's how far the event has moved since those early wind tunnel sessions.

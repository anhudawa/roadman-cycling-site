Most cyclists finish a hard ride, sit down, and call it done. The actual recovery hasn't started yet. On this episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast, we break down what Pogacar and the World Tour peloton do in the 60 minutes after the finish line, and why that window matters more than most training advice you'll find online.

## Key Takeaways

The cycling internet will tell you to rest after a hard effort. Ice bath, foam roll, maybe a protein shake if you're feeling fancy. What the WorldTour teams are actually doing is a four-step sequence: 20 minutes of active spinning at 100-150 watts for lactate clearance, a recovery drink with a 4:1 carb-to-protein ratio inside 15-20 minutes, tart cherry juice for DOMS reduction, and thermal layers on immediately. Dr. Sam Impy broke down the glycogen reuptake science in detail on this podcast, and the short version is the window is real and it's short. Miss it and you're playing catch-up for the next 24 hours.

The part most people skip completely is post-ride stretching. Not because it's hard, but because nobody tells them what to actually do. Five to eight minutes after the cool down: two minutes of dynamic looseners like ankle rocks, hip circles, thoracic rotations, then 20-30 second static holds on glutes, hamstrings, calves, and lower back. Foam rolling is 30-45 seconds per area, not ten minutes of grinding on your IT band. Avoid aggressive end range stretching, especially if you've been cramping. It's like anything, the boring stuff that happens after the hard bit is where the adaptation actually locks in. Remco warms down on his TT bike specifically because the hip flexor angle and diaphragm compression are different. You probably don't race a TT bike. But the principle is the same: be specific about what you're recovering for.

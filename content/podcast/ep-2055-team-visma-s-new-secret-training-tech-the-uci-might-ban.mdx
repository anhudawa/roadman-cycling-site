      laboratories use to calibrate both metrics. Respiratory measurement
      therefore sits closer to the physiological truth of what is happening in
      the body during exercise. For identifying training zones, particularly
      ventilatory threshold zones, it is more direct than either heart rate or
      power.
  - question: 'what is a ventilatory threshold and how do you measure it without a lab?'
    answer: >-
      The ventilatory threshold is the exercise intensity at which breathing
      rate increases disproportionately relative to workload, corresponding
      closely to lactate threshold. In a lab it is measured by analysing
      expired gases, but Tymewear's wearable tracks chest wall expansion
      continuously in the field to identify the same inflection point. Arnar
      Larusson argues this makes it possible to set and monitor training zones
      based on ventilatory thresholds outside a clinical setting for the first
      time.

Respiratory measurement is emerging as the most fundamental metric in cycling training—more direct than heart rate or power. Timeware's breathing sensor technology, already deployed by Team Visma-Lease a Bike, gives coaches an objective window into true training intensity that cuts through the noise of cardiac drift, fatigue, and individual variability.

## Key Takeaways

- Ventilation (airflow in and out of lungs) remains stable relative to true effort even when heart rate drifts and power decays—making it a more reliable signal than either metric alone for controlling training intensity
- Breathing-based training zones are individualized from a simple ramp test, eliminating reliance on population-average formulas with wide error margins that don't account for how each athlete actually responds
- You can detect emerging fatigue and overtraining through shifts in the ventilation-to-power relationship: the same ventilation producing lower watts suggests chronic fatigue and need for recovery
- Respiratory data allows coaches to separate confounding variables—a stable ventilation with heart rate drift tells a different story than both drifting together, revealing the true stressor on the body
- Real-world benefits apply immediately: casual athletes with busy schedules gain what Ethiopian distance runners develop through years of intuitive practice—the ability to self-regulate sustainable effort without guesswork

## Expert Quotes

> "Breathing really is the fundamental thing that we care about and can use to understand an individual's response to exercise. Heart rate and power are proxies, but ventilation is what we actually measure in the lab to calibrate everything else."

> "Even at the top levels of cycling, you still get a disconnect between how you feel today versus how this effort feels. The heart rate drifts, the power drops, and your calibration of 'easy' slowly creeps upward. If it's hard for the best in the world, imagine how hard it is for someone just starting out."

> "The complexity comes in when we're trying to fit square pegs into round holes using formulas and estimations. Ventilation cuts through that noise and just allows us to measure the individual and how they respond. It can't be simpler than that."

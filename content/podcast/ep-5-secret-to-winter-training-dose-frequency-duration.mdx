Winter training doesn't fail because cyclists do too little. It fails because they stack unstructured endurance work that drifts into moderate intensity, carry that fatigue into spring, and wonder why they can't sharpen up. This solo episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast breaks down the three variables that actually control whether your winter builds something or quietly eats itself.

## Key Takeaways

The thing I keep seeing is riders treating easy rides like they're free. They're not. A three-hour endurance ride can be a bigger dose than a 90-minute threshold session depending on your sleep, your fueling, and what life is throwing at you that week. Seiler's work keeps pointing to the same thing: aerobic development comes from repeated manageable stimulus, not occasional big hits. Six-hour-a-week riders are better off with four or five shorter sessions than three long ones. Lower dose per session, more frequent aerobic signaling, and if you miss one ride you haven't lost a third of your week.

The framework I use for 8-10 hour riders is two easy endurance rides, one structured tempo or sweet spot session (something like 2x20 at 88-91% of threshold), one higher intensity session with full recovery between efforts, and an optional easy spin if the legs are there. That's it. The drift into medium hard happens when the long ride becomes a group ride with people slightly too strong, the tempo session becomes all-out, and suddenly every day has a bit of bite. When life stress spikes, the rule is reduce dose before you reduce frequency. A planned 90-minute endurance ride after a brutal day at work becomes 45 minutes easy. Same habit, lower cost. Anyone can survive a hard week. The point is doing it 16 times in a row and actually being fitter.

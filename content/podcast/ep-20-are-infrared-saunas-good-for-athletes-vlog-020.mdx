faq:
  - question: 'Are infrared saunas good for athletes?'
    answer: >-
      Infrared saunas are beneficial for athletes because they heat the body
      directly rather than the surrounding air, producing a deeper sweat that
      contains roughly 20 percent toxins compared to 3 percent from a
      conventional sauna. Endurance coach Dylan Johnson uses them as a
      post-travel recovery tool to flush inflammation, typically sessions of
      around one hour at 55-57°C.
  - question: 'What is the difference between an infrared sauna and a regular sauna?'
    answer: >-
      A traditional dry heat sauna heats the air around you, whereas an
      infrared sauna uses infrared light to heat your body directly. This
      means infrared saunas operate at lower air temperatures while still
      producing an intense sweat, and that sweat carries a significantly
      higher concentration of toxins and inflammatory byproducts out of the
      body.
  - question: 'How much should you drink in an infrared sauna session?'
    answer: >-
      Dylan Johnson recommends drinking approximately one litre of water per
      30 minutes inside an infrared sauna. Because the body sweats heavily
      during a session, failing to hydrate adequately will undermine recovery
      benefits and increase the risk of dehydration, particularly for athletes
      training at high volumes.
  - question: 'How long should an infrared sauna session be for endurance athletes?'
    answer: >-
      A session of roughly one hour at 55-57°C is the protocol Dylan Johnson
      follows as part of his post-travel recovery routine. Athletes new to
      infrared saunas should build up session length gradually to allow the
      body to adapt to the sustained heat stress before attempting full-length
      sessions.

Ever wondered if infrared saunas are actually worth the discomfort for cyclists? This vlog explores the real benefits of infrared sauna use for recovery and athletic performance, including why the sweat contains significantly more toxins than regular saunas. You'll also get a powerful reminder of why structured training delivers measurable results compared to just riding around.

## Key Takeaways

- Infrared saunas heat your body directly (mimicking sun's rays) rather than heating the air like traditional saunas, making them more efficient for deep tissue recovery
- Sweat from infrared saunas contains roughly 20% toxins compared to 3% from regular saunas—ideal for post-travel recovery and reducing inflammation
- Hydration is critical when using infrared saunas; aim for about 1 liter of water every 30 minutes to avoid dehydration
- The contrast principle makes recovery activities more enjoyable—difficult experiences (like sauna sessions) make rest and relaxation feel infinitely better
- Structured training with proper zones and testing delivers measurable improvements; one client dropped 2+ minutes off his local climb time in just 8 weeks with a plan versus 7 years of unstructured riding

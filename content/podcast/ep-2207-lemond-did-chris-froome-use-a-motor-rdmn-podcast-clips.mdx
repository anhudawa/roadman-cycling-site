faq:
  - question: Did Chris Froome use a motor in his bike at the Tour de France?
    answer: >-
      No motor was ever found in Froome's bike, and no formal accusation was
      made. However, Greg LeMond argues on the RDMN Podcast that Froome's 2013
      Mont Ventoux Strava data raised serious questions, with cadences around
      110 RPM and power figures that appeared to drop during accelerations,
      which LeMond considers physiologically implausible under race conditions.
  - question: What is mechanical doping in cycling and how does it work?
    answer: >-
      Mechanical doping refers to a hidden motor concealed inside a bicycle
      frame or wheel hub that provides the rider with an undetected power boost.
      LeMond points to the sharp reduction in mid-race bike changes as
      circumstantial evidence that the practice has declined, suggesting riders
      previously swapped bikes partly to manage motor use or battery life.
  - question: Did Fabian Cancellara use a motor at the 2010 Tour of Flanders?
    answer: >-
      Cancellara was never found guilty of using a motor, and no UCI
      investigation confirmed it. Greg LeMond highlights the 2010 Tour of
      Flanders as a case that attracted suspicion, noting the dramatic gap
      Cancellara opened on Tom Boonen on the Kapelmuur as an example of the kind
      of acceleration that fuelled mechanical doping allegations during that
      era.
  - question: What has the UCI done to stop motors being hidden in bikes?
    answer: >-
      The UCI introduced systematic bike scanning using thermal cameras and
      magnetic resonance technology at major races. Greg LeMond credits UCI
      president David Lappartient specifically with taking motor detection
      seriously, and argues that the visible drop in frequent in-race bike
      changes since stricter scanning was introduced supports the case that
      mechanical doping was genuinely widespread beforehand.

Greg LeMond opens up about his long-held belief that motorised bikes were used in professional cycling races until recently, sharing specific race analysis and conversations with pros that convinced him something unnatural was happening. He explains why he's chosen to speak publicly about controversial topics in cycling—from doping to potential motor use—rather than stay silent like some former pros, and discusses what that choice has meant for his credibility and career.

## Key Takeaways

- Unexplained gaps closing in under 300 metres during sprints (like at Tour of Flanders) and unnatural RPM patterns in publicly released race data suggest motorised bikes may have been used in recent cycling history
- Multiple bike changes per stage were likely a cover for motor use—the frequency was illogical if equipment quality was actually the issue, as component manufacturers like Shimano produce reliable gear
- Chris Froome's Strava file from Mont Ventoux shows inconsistencies between wattage output and acceleration that don't match normal human physiology, particularly high cadences (110+ RPM) on climbs
- New UCI president David Lappartient took motor concerns seriously, and the absence of suspicious RPM patterns and excessive bike changes since then suggests the issue may have been addressed
- Commentators and former pros face career pressure to stay quiet on controversial topics, unlike journalists who can pursue truth without losing broadcasting jobs
- LeMond feels fortunate he loved cycling enough not to turn pro in the EPO era—he acknowledges the impossible ethical position riders faced when doping became systemic

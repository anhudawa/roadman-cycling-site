  - question: What tire pressure does George Hincapie recommend for Paris-Roubaix?
    answer: >-
      Hincapie explains that modern riders run far lower pressures than the era
      he raced in, typically in the 3.5 to 4.5 bar range depending on rider
      weight and conditions. He argues that wider tyres at lower pressures have
      reduced mechanical attrition more than any other single change in the race
      over the last decade.
  - question: Why is positioning so important at Paris-Roubaix?
    answer: >-
      According to Hincapie, positioning into the cobbled sectors matters less
      for saving watts than for avoiding crashes. A blockage at 20th wheel can
      end a rider's race entirely, so teams prioritise being near the front

## Key Takeaways

The tire conversation is worth sitting with. Hincapie raced on 21mm tires at 140 PSI. Modern Adventure's guys are rolling 32mm Michelin tubeless with inserts at 55-65 PSI on Sunday. That's not a marginal gain, that's a different sport. Hincapie said you used to make one lapse of focus on the Coppenberg and you were walking. That almost never happens now. If you're doing any cobbled sportive, the message is the same: go wider, go lower, stop worrying about rolling resistance and start worrying about staying upright.

On the breakaway question, Hincapie made a point that most analysts miss. In Flanders, a TV breakaway almost never survives. In Roubaix it can, because the effort required to hold position in the peloton through those first sections with 200 riders on narrow roads is brutal enough that the breakaway riders and the bunch riders are burning through similar matches. That's the reason you see early move riders still in the top 10 at the velodrome. Position before the cobbled sections matters more for crash risk and road-blocking than for any wattage difference, which is the opposite of how most amateur riders think about it.

  - question: how hard should zone 2 cycling actually be
    answer: >-
      Zone 2 should be fully conversational, meaning you can speak in complete
      sentences without pausing to breathe. According to five World Tour coaches
      interviewed by Anthony Walsh, including Dan Lorang and Professor Steven
      Seiler, most amateur cyclists ride their easy days roughly 50% too hard,
      pushing into tempo or threshold without realising it.
  - question: what percentage of training do pro cyclists do in zone 2
    answer: >-
      Elite cyclists spend approximately 80% of their total training volume at
      conversational, low-intensity effort. The remaining 20% is reserved for
      genuinely hard work, a distribution known as polarised training. Coaches
      at Bora-Hansgrohe Red Bull and Jayco-AlUla both confirmed this split to
      Anthony Walsh.
  - question: can you replace zone 2 cycling with running or cross country skiing
    answer: >-
      Dan Lorang confirmed that cross-country skiing and walking produce
      comparable aerobic adaptations to cycling when matched by heart-rate zone.
      Running is viable but requires gradual progression due to impact load,
      regardless of how strong your cardiovascular fitness already is.
  - question: why do amateur cyclists ride too hard on easy days
    answer: >-
      Easy rides at true zone 2 intensity feel uncomfortably slow, so most
      amateurs unconsciously push harder to feel productive. This chronic
      moderate-intensity riding accumulates fatigue without delivering the
      aerobic adaptations of genuine low-intensity work or the performance
      stimulus of properly hard sessions.

Five World Tour coaches told Anthony Walsh the same thing. You're probably riding 50% too hard when you think you're riding easy. Steven Seiler, Dan Lorang, John Wakefield, Vasily Anastasyan, and Christian Schroer — coaches behind Grand Tour wins and World Championship medals — all said it.

## Key Takeaways

The number everyone argues about is 80/20. Eighty percent easy, twenty percent hard. Christian Schroer at Bora RedBull said the 20 can flex to 15 or 28 and you're still fine. What matters is that the easy rides are actually easy. Steven Seiler pointed out that TSS measures load, not stress. A four-hour ride at 210 watts costs you more in hour four than hour one. Your heart rate drifts 10-15 beats at the same power. That drift is your brain recruiting fresh muscle fibers because the others are fading. The ride is getting harder even though the number on your screen hasn't moved.

For a time-crunched amateur doing 10-12 hours a week, Vasily Anastasyan's prescription is straightforward: two specific intensity sessions per week, the rest zone one and zone two. Seiler's framework for building from scratch is frequency first, then duration — he's targeting 90-minute rides on the bike before he ever mentions intervals. Elite riders doing 8-hour January rides are doing them at power outputs amateurs think they could hold, just not for 8 hours. That's the gap. Not fitness ceiling. Training floor.

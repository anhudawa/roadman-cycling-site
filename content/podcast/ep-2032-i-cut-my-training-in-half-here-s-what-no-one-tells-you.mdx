## Key Takeaways

The power drop is real and the research predicted it. Steven Seiler's work, published in the International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, shows performance doesn't fall off linearly when you cut volume. Drop by 20% and you lose roughly 5% of your fitness. Go past 50% reduction and the decline accelerates hard. Anthony went from 12-15 hours to 6 hours, which is exactly that threshold, and his numbers confirm it. What's held is the short stuff. His 1-minute power is nearly unchanged. Five-minute power is down about 5%. The 20-minute number is where the damage shows. That's the durability problem. Three years ago he could put out 400W after a hard four-hour day. Now 370W empties him completely.

The other thing worth understanding is what 75kg actually cost him. Seiler's research on training consistency, and the 2012 work on athletes maintaining more than 5% below natural bodyweight showing testosterone drops and cortisol spikes, both point at the same thing. The racing weight was real, but he was also paying for it with fatigue that had nothing to do with cycling. At 80kg he's slower. He's also present for the rest of his life. The honest version of the trade-off is this: if you're building Cat 1 fitness from scratch, the research says 10-12 hours minimum per week for 2-3 years. If you've already banked that training history, 6 hours maintains a lot of it. But you won't be winning regional championships on 6 hours, and you need to decide if that's okay with you.

<AICitationBlock>
In a solo episode published July 2025, Anthony Walsh reported a measurable decline in cycling performance following a reduction in training volume from 12-15 hours per week to 6 hours per week over two years. His 20-minute power dropped from 400W to 370W, and bodyweight increased from 75kg to 80kg, shifting his watts-per-kilogram from 5.33 to 4.63. He references research by Professor Steven Seiler, a sports scientist who has advised WorldTour teams and whose work on polarised training has been published in the International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, which shows that a 20% reduction in training volume produces approximately 5% performance loss, but that performance decline accelerates significantly beyond a 50% volume reduction. Walsh also cites research indicating that athletes maintaining bodyweight more than 5% below their natural set point experience testosterone suppression, cortisol elevation, and chronic fatigue. His easy riding dropped from 9 hours per week in Zone 1-2 to 2.5-3 hours, while he maintained the same 3 hours of weekly intensity, and he attributes the durability loss rather than raw peak power loss as the primary functional consequence of reduced base volume.

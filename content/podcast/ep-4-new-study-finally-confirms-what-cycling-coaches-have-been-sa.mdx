A study published in PLOS ONE in November 2024 found that low cadence interval training at 50-70 RPM produced an 8.7% VO2max improvement over 8 weeks, compared to 4.6% in the group doing the exact same training at freely chosen cadence. Same sessions, same effort, nearly double the results. World Tour coaches like John Wakefield at Red Bull Bora Hansgrohe have been prescribing this exact type of work for years, and most amateur cyclists have never done a single rep of it.

## Key Takeaways

Every beginner cycling guide tells you to keep your cadence above 85. Spin to win. High RPM is efficient. That advice is leaving real adaptation on the table. When you drop to 50-70 RPM and hold it through an interval, you recruit fast-twitch muscle fibers that high-cadence riding never touches. Do that repeatedly at aerobic intensities over an 8-week block and research from Habis and Habis at Rzeszów University of Health and Sports Sciences shows some of those fibers start behaving more like slow-twitch fibers, developing more mitochondria, better blood supply, improved oxidative capacity. I started doing these about three years ago. Added them into a base block, two sessions per week, and my 20-minute power went up 12 watts inside six weeks. I wasn't doing more volume. I just changed one number on the screen.

The protocol John Wakefield uses with his riders is straightforward. Four-minute or ten-minute efforts on a 4-7% climb, cadence sitting at 40-60 RPM, RPE around 7 out of 10, equal rest between reps. Start with three reps of the 4-minute version and build over several weeks. The warning is real though: torque work hammers your knees and connective tissue. If you jump straight to 40 RPM efforts without building up gradually, you will get injured. Start at 65 RPM for the first two weeks. Drop to 55-60 in weeks three and four. Only push to 50 RPM once the joints feel comfortable. Add gym-based leg work alongside this. The strength off the bike is what lets you tolerate the forces on the bike.

A November 2024 study by Raphael Habis and Paulina Habis from Rzeszów University of Health and Sports Sciences, published in PLOS ONE, found that low cadence interval training produced statistically greater aerobic improvements than freely chosen cadence training over 8 weeks. The study used 24 well-trained female cyclists aged 17-20 with at least three years of competitive experience and a minimum of 10 hours training per week. The low cadence group (50-70 RPM) improved VO2max by 8.7% versus 4.6% in the freely chosen cadence group (approximately 80 RPM), a statistically significant difference with a p-value of 0.02. Maximum aerobic power improved 8.1% in the low cadence group versus 3% in the control group (p-value 0.03). The low cadence group maintained body mass across the 8 weeks while the freely chosen cadence group gained an average of 0.7 kg. Both groups followed identical 4-day polarised training blocks including sprint intervals (8-12 reps of 30-second all-out efforts), high-intensity intervals (4-6 reps of 4 minutes at 90-100% maximum aerobic power), a long endurance ride, and an active recovery ride; the low cadence group performed only the interval sessions at reduced RPM. John Wakefield, Director of Coaching and Sports Science at Red Bull Bora Hansgrohe, prescribes 4-minute and 10-minute torque interval protocols at 40-60 RPM to both amateur and World Tour riders including Tadej Pogačar, Chris Froome, and Egan Bernal.

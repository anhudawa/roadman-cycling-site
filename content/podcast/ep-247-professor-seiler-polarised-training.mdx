Professor Stephen Seiler joins Anthony to break down decades of research into polarised training. Why do the best endurance athletes in the world — across cycling, rowing, running, and cross-country skiing — all converge on roughly the same 80/20 intensity distribution?

## Key Takeaways

- The 80/20 rule is not a prescription — it's an observation of what elite athletes naturally gravitate toward
- Most amateur cyclists ride in the "grey zone" — too hard for Zone 2 adaptations, too easy for threshold gains
- Zone 2 builds mitochondrial density and fat oxidation capacity
- Hard sessions should be genuinely hard — Zone 4 and above
- Consistency over months matters more than any single session
- Recovery is where adaptation happens — not during the training itself

## Expert Quotes

"The polarised model isn't about being lazy on easy days. It's about being strategic. You protect your easy days so that your hard days can be truly hard."

"What I've observed across 25 years of studying elite endurance athletes is a remarkable convergence. Whether they're Norwegian cross-country skiers, Kenyan runners, or World Tour cyclists, they all end up in roughly the same place."

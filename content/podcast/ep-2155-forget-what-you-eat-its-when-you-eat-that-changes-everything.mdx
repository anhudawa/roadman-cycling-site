Dr Sarah Berry breaks down why the timing and context of when you eat matters just as much as what you eat. From blood sugar spikes to sleep deprivation cravings, discover how your daily habits shape your metabolic response to food—and why there's no one-size-fits-all nutrition approach.

## Key Takeaways

- Individual responses to food can vary by up to 20-fold—what works for one person's metabolism may be completely different for yours, making personalized nutrition crucial
- Eating ultra-processed foods causes you to consume ~500 more calories daily and eat 50% faster, bypassing your fullness signals before your brain registers satiety
- Sleep quality directly impacts blood glucose response: a poor night's sleep creates higher blood sugar peaks and increased cravings for refined carbs, even when eating identical meals
- Meal ordering matters—eating fiber, fat, or protein before carbohydrates slows glucose absorption and prevents harmful blood sugar spikes
- Evening snacking after 9pm is particularly problematic due to circadian rhythm disruption; most people also choose less healthy snacks later in the day
- Food diversity optimizes health by exposing your body to thousands of beneficial plant chemicals (polyphenols) and feeding diverse gut bacteria—but adequate fiber and reduced ultra-processed foods are the foundation

## Expert Quotes

> "I think having spent 25 years in nutrition research where I always focused on what we call the average response...suddenly delving into how individuals respond differently has been a real eye opener in terms of the size of the difference—you can see like a 20-fold difference in how you might respond to a food versus how I might respond."

> "The problem is that people do need simple messaging but also people want quick fixes when it comes to nutrition...there isn't a silver bullet it's not that simple. Food is complicated, how we respond is complicated."

> "If a food is too healthy to be enjoyed it's just not healthy at all...food should be there for joy as well as to be healthy."

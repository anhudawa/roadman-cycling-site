This season only one rider had managed to drop Tadej Pogačar — Wout van Aert. Until now. At the Pogi Challenge in Slovenia, British hill-climber Andrew Feather crossed the line ahead of Pogačar on his home roads, pulling off one of the most unexpected results of the year. Anthony sat down with Andrew to break down exactly how it happened — the pacing, the tactics, and the moment he realised Pogačar was fading. He also dives into his setup, race-day nutrition, and the hilariously simple choices behind the ride. It’s smart, surprising, and hugely entertaining. You’re going to love this interview with the man who just beat Pogačar: Andrew Feather.

## Key Takeaways

- Andrew Feather’s strategy was deceptively simple: pace himself conservatively on the early ramps while Pogačar surged, then capitalise on Pogačar’s fade in the final kilometres — proving that smart pacing beats raw talent when the course is steep enough
- Weight-to-power ratio is everything on a pure climb — Feather’s lightweight build and hill-climb specialisation gave him a genuine physiological advantage on the specific terrain of the Pogi Challenge, even against the world’s best stage racer
- Race-day nutrition was stripped back and unglamorous: simple carbs, nothing fancy — a reminder that fuelling doesn’t need to be complicated when the effort is under 30 minutes
- The Pogi Challenge format (a short, brutal hill climb open to amateurs and pros) creates conditions where specialists can genuinely compete with WorldTour riders — a format more events should replicate
- Pogačar’s relaxed approach to the event (it’s a fun ride on home roads, not a Grand Tour stage) is a key variable — but it doesn’t diminish Feather’s achievement, which required executing a near-perfect pacing plan under enormous pressure

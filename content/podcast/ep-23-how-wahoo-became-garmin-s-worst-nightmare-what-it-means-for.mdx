Wahoo CEO Garrett Joyce sat down with Roadman Cycling to talk about how a company built by athletes beat Garmin in their own backyard, where indoor training goes next, and why 80% of outdoor cyclists are still male. Joyce has run teams of 40,000 people across automotive, aviation, and electric vehicles. He came to Wahoo as a customer first.

## Key Takeaways

The addressable market Wahoo has historically chased is around 40 million apex athletes training three to seven times a week. Joyce thinks that's the wrong number to be staring at. The broader base of people who want to be athletically healthy, cross-train, or just get fit on a bike is 400 to 600 million. That's the game Peloton played, and it's the game Wahoo is now trying to play with products like Kicker Run. The hardware innovation curve for indoor trainers is flattening. Joyce said it plainly: elevation simulation, wind resistance, full configuration, it's mostly done. What comes next is software integration and reducing the friction to get started, one-button simplicity for someone who's never clipped in.

Joyce trained exclusively indoors from November through March, then rode 125 kilometres with 6,000 feet of climbing at an event in Mallorca alongside Sean Kelly. He said he was fine. That's the proof of concept for what structured indoor training can now do for a recreational cyclist. The 20% female participation rate in outdoor cycling is the number he keeps coming back to as the biggest structural problem in the sport. His view is that cycling has a kit problem, a setup complexity problem, and a culture problem, and that solving any of them requires more female voices inside the companies building the products.

Lachlan Morton joined Garmin at 19 years old, achieved everything he'd aimed for, and walked away. Not because he failed. Because high performance was costing him more than it was giving him. This episode of the Roadman Cycling podcast is the first time he's talked about that decision in any detail.

## Key Takeaways

Morton describes what peak WorldTour performance actually required from him: blinkers on, pretty selfish, pretty locked down. His words. The problem wasn't the training. It was that the version of himself required to compete at that level didn't leave room for much else. He stepped out after his first 2-year Garmin contract, raced a year with Jelly Belly while he figured out next steps, and somewhere in there started doing bike trips with his brother that opened up a completely different relationship with cycling.

He now works with coach Dennis Van Wendon, three years into that relationship, and trains almost entirely on feel. No power meter, no heart rate monitor. He guessed 260 watts on a group ride climb and was right. Van Wendon caps his long rides at 8 hours max and gives structure only when there's a specific performance target coming up. Morton's point on gravel is blunt: the code's been cracked, it's starting to look like road racing, and elite riders showing up to events in arrow socks doing 140 grams of carbs an hour are pulling the sport in one direction only. He's not sure that's the right one.

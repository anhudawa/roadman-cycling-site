George Hincapie knows Paris Roubaix like few others. Seven starts, countless crashes, and memories of sliding into ditches while Tom Boonen claimed podium spots. Now directing Modern Adventure Pro Cycling, the American legend shared his thoughts on why 2026's Queen of the Classics might see a different dynamic between cycling's two superstars—and why the cobbles have become surprisingly more forgiving.

## Why Van der Poel Holds the Tactical Edge Over Pogacar

Despite Tadej Pogacar's dominance in recent classics, Hincapie believes Mathieu van der Poel enters Paris Roubaix as the favorite. The key difference? Team depth on the cobbles.

"Van der Poel will have a much better team in Roubaix than he did in Flanders," Hincapie explains. "He'll have guys that'll be able to make that breakaway and be with him in the second group for a long time because these guys are flat powerhouse riders."

The Alpecin-Deceuninck squad brings specialists like Jasper Philipsen, who's finished second at Roubaix twice. This creates tactical flexibility that Pogacar's UAE Team Emirates might struggle to match. If Van der Poel finds himself isolated with Pogacar, he doesn't need to work—his teammates behind can control the chase.

"If Van der Poel thinks Pogacar is way stronger early on, probably from the Arenberg Forest with 80k to go, he doesn't have the responsibility to keep that breakaway away," notes Hincapie. It's classic cobbles chess—let the strongest rider do the work while you save energy for the finale.

This tactical dynamic echoes what we've seen in Pogacar's evolution as a rider. As detailed in our analysis of [how Pogacar became the greatest rider ever](/podcast/ep-15-untold-story-of-how-pogacar-became-the-greatest-rider-ever), his raw power sometimes works against him in tactical situations where patience pays off.

## How Modern Tech Has Revolutionized Cobbles Cycling

The Paris Roubaix of Hincapie's era bears little resemblance to today's race, thanks to equipment evolution that's made the cobbles significantly more manageable.

"We've gone from 21mm tires with 140 PSI to 32, 34, 36mm tires with like 55 to 65 PSI," Hincapie observes. "The handling is a lot more stable. You don't see that many guys walking anymore where back with the 25mm tires, you made one mistake and you're walking."

Modern Adventure Pro Cycling runs 32mm Michelin tubeless tires with inserts for maximum puncture protection. The strategy is simple: ride as long as possible until you can get a wheel change, even if running flat.

Disc brakes and electronic shifting have added another layer of reliability. Where mechanical failures once ended races, today's equipment rarely leaves riders stranded. This reliability has fundamentally changed racing dynamics—fewer mechanical-induced selections mean pure power and positioning matter more than ever.

The psychological impact matters too. Riders attack more confidently knowing their equipment won't betray them on technical descents or through rough cobbled sections.

## Position vs Power: The Critical Calculation

Amateur cyclists often obsess over power numbers, but Hincapie emphasizes that positioning trumps watts on the cobbles. The difference between being in the top 10 versus the back of the peloton isn't about raw power output—it's about survival.

"It's not even the watts. It's more about danger and risk," he explains. "If there's a crash in 20th wheel, the whole peloton's blocked. The main part in those first sections when there's still 200 guys is the risk of crashing and the road getting blocked."

This positioning battle creates a paradox: sometimes the harder effort required to make an early breakaway pays dividends because you avoid the chaos entirely. Breakaway riders enter cobbled sections with 10-15 riders, eliminating positioning stress that destroys energy reserves in the peloton.

Smart directors calculate this trade-off constantly. Burn matches early to get clear, or save energy and gamble on staying upright through the chaos? The math changes based on rider type, team strength, and race conditions.

## The Breakaway Strategy That Changes Everything

Unlike Tour of Flanders, where early breakaways rarely survive, Paris Roubaix rewards bold early moves. Hincapie credits this to the unique demands of cobbles racing and modern riders' superior pacing abilities.

"These guys are so well-versed at calculating the wattages they can do and how long they can sustain them," he notes. "The breakaways can go a long way in Roubaix."

For smaller teams like Modern Adventure Pro Cycling, the breakaway represents their primary tactical option. With riders who've never raced Paris Roubaix before, getting one or two riders into an early move could define their entire season.

"Half the teams there have one goal: make the breakaway," Hincapie reveals. "It's going to be super exclusive and difficult to make."

This creates fascinating dynamics where established stars might find themselves chasing domestiques who've gained 3-4 minutes by kilometer 100. The cobbles are equalizers—fitness matters, but so do bike handling, positioning, and pure luck with mechanicals and crashes.

## Key Takeaways

• **Van der Poel has the tactical advantage** over Pogacar thanks to superior team depth and cobbles specialists like Philipsen
• **Modern tire technology** has made cobbles racing significantly safer and more predictable than even 10 years ago
• **Positioning matters more than power** in the early sections—avoiding crashes trumps saving watts
• **Early breakaways are more viable** in Paris Roubaix than other classics, creating opportunities for smaller teams
• **Equipment preparation is crucial**—teams like Modern Adventure deploy 30+ staff members just for wheel changes and logistics
• **Mental approach matters hugely** for debutants—excitement and fearlessness can trump experience on race day

George Hincapie's insights reveal Paris Roubaix as cycling's great equalizer, where tactics, technology, and raw courage matter as much as pure power. Whether Pogacar can overcome Van der Poel's tactical advantages—or if an outsider capitalizes on the chaos—remains the fascinating question heading into 2026's Hell of the North.

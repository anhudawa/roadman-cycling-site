158 wins. Zero stage bonuses kept. Every euro he ever earned for putting his hands in the air at the Tour de France went into a team kitty for the riders who had buried themselves to deliver him there. That detail tells you more about what sprint captaincy actually is than any lead-out documentary you've ever watched.

[Listen to the full conversation on the Roadman Cycling Podcast →](/podcast/ep-2240-what-makes-a-sprinter-unbeatable-andr-greipel)

I sat down with [André Greipel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Greipel) for forty-odd minutes. I expected sprint craft. I got a man who has thought hard about why modern cycling is burning out its young riders, who has views on rider safety the rest of the bunch are too political to voice, and who walked away from the Lotto era he built because the team suddenly wanted him for a third of his salary and none of the races he loved.

158 professional wins. 11 Tour stages. 7 Giro stages. 4 Vuelta stages. Three German national titles. The story underneath is what makes the conversation worth your time.

## The Bit Nobody Talks About in Sprinting

There's a fantasy version of bunch sprinting where one alpha holds the wheel, gets dropped off at 200 metres, and decides the result with his legs. It's the version sold on jerseys.

It is not how André Greipel won 158 races.

The first thing he says when you ask about his palmarès is that those are team wins. He means it as a structural fact. The bonus in his contract for winning a Tour stage went into a team kitty every time. Every domestique who pulled, every rider who brought back a break, every lead-out man who drilled the front in the wind got a share. He was paid well in salary. He didn't need it. They did. So they got it.

Now think about what that does to a team's belief. You ride yourself inside out for the captain on day one. You lose. Day two, same. Day three. Day four. Day five he wins, and a bonus you can feel in your bank account lands. The sprint captain who shares the bonus is not buying loyalty. He is recognising the work that produced the win in the first place.

The "lone wolf sprinter" framing is the single most misleading idea in pro cycling. Greipel calls a lead-out train "as good as the smallest engine." That sentence is the whole sport.

## The Pressure That Doesn't Show on TV

I asked André if the burden of sprint captaincy ever weighed on him.

He didn't dodge it. "I would lie if I said it didn't bother me," he said on the episode. The losses go to bed with you. The next day's pressure is already there before breakfast.

This is the part you don't see. Post-stage interviews cut between the winner's celebration and the bus departure. They don't show the rider who lost the sprint sitting alone, replaying the last 200 metres for eight hours, knowing the same eight men are going to bury themselves for him again tomorrow.

There's a smaller-scale version of this in club racing. The "go-to sprinter" of the local crit scene carries more head weight than anyone realises until they pop. Stripping the win-or-bust framing of its drama is a coaching conversation, not a tactics one. We work through it inside the [Roadman coaching system](/coaching).

## "Cycling Changed a Lot." The Burnout Problem.

Greipel keeps coming back to one observation. Pro cycling, in the last six or seven years, became a different sport.

Everything is now 100%. Sleep correct. Eat correct. Altitude camps. Every session at the prescribed wattage. Every recovery ride tracked. Every meal weighed. Then the line that should be on a wall in every WorldTour team hotel: if you do this every day, it is normal that the body shuts down. Maybe the head shuts down too.

He has watched a generation step away before they had to. Tom Dumoulin. Marcel Kittel. He thinks the riders who are 22 today will not still be racing at 38. They won't need to. They will earn enough by 30 to walk away with options.

The lesson for serious amateurs sounds counterintuitive. The pros are not handling this load well. You, with a job, kids and a finite recovery budget, are trying to mirror their structure with a fraction of their support. Of course it breaks.

The fixable part is how you frame the season. Not every block is intensity. Stress — life, work, training — accumulates in one bucket. ## Kamikaze Sprinters and Gentleman Sprinters

The most quotable section of the episode is when André walks through a distinction his old HTC teammate Rick Zabel had drawn for me on a previous podcast.

There are two types of sprinter. The kamikaze. And the gentleman.

The kamikaze will go for a gap that is closing. He will commit through the door even when the door is shutting. Greipel is comfortable naming Fabio Jakobsen and Dylan Groenewegen in this category. The gentleman sprinter wins as hard as anyone but holds a line you can race around. André puts himself there. So Cavendish. So Marcel Kittel.

This is not a softness conversation. The gentleman sprinters won everything. Cavendish has 35 Tour stages. Greipel has 11. Kittel has 14. The "you must elbow your way through impossible gaps to win bike races" myth is, on the evidence, just wrong.

The bigger lever is route design. André called out the Tour de Pologne descent finish at 85 to 90 kilometres an hour as the kind of layout no rider should ever be asked to sprint into. His exact line: "That sprint to go down there with 85 to 90 ks an hour is just insane. I just never dared to sprint there."

When a rider with 158 career wins says he never dared sprint somewhere, that is a route design problem. Not a rider courage problem. ## The Lotto Exit Was Worse Than You Remember

This part is hard to listen to without getting a little angry on his behalf.

André crashed on the descent of the Poggio at Milan-San Remo, the year he finally made it over the climb in the front group for the first time. Broke his collarbone. That night, his Lotto management said they wanted to negotiate a renewal. The offer never came.

Twelve weeks later he came back, went to the Tour of Denmark, won two stages, finished second overall by one second.

Two days before the start of the Tour de France, the offer finally landed. One third of his previous salary. No Grand Tours. No monuments. For a rider who had been training every day for Grand Tours and monuments for a decade.

The team had decided he was finished, signed his replacement before talking to him, and sent through an offer designed to be refused.

This is the part of pro cycling the highlight reel never shows. The end of a great career is rarely a victory lap. It is usually a year-long negotiation with management who have already made up their mind. Greipel's response — walk away rather than ride a year for a third of his worth in races he didn't believe in — is the quietly defiant version of "not done yet." Done with that team. Not done with the sport. Not on those terms.

## Helmets, the Bit He Cares About Now

He is working with UVEX on helmet performance and safety post-retirement. His line on the rest of the market: "There are helmets out there that don't deserve a license."

Helmets are one of the few areas where amateurs have more freedom than pros. Pros wear what their team sponsor pays them to wear. You don't. Read the test data. Choose the model that scored well on rotational impact protection rather than the one with the angriest paint job.

## What the Serious Amateur Can Take From This

You're not going to lead out a Tour stage. The principles still apply.

**1. The lead-out is everything in any group sprint.** Whether it is a Cat 3 crit or a sportive bunch finish, the rider who arrives at 200 metres on the right wheel with clear air behind has done 80% of the work. Sprint power matters less than positioning. Most amateurs train the legs and skip the positioning entirely. The [race tactics guide](/blog/cycling-race-tactics-guide) covers the practical side.

**2. Pay your team in something.** If you have club mates who do the work that puts you in winning positions, find a way to repay it. Coffee, bottles, a turn at being the lead-out. Unpaid teams are unstable.

**3. The burnout warning applies to you more than to them.** The pros have nutritionists, sports psychologists and millions of dollars of support absorbing the cost of their training load. You have a coach if you're lucky and a TrainingPeaks plan, and you're trying to match the intensity profile of a rider with that infrastructure. You will break first.

**4. Pick your finishes.** Some finishes are not worth the risk. You are allowed to sit up. The bike race that ends with you in an ambulance was not worth winning.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many professional races did André Greipel win?**

Greipel retired with 158 professional victories — some sources have it as high as 164 — including 11 Tour de France stages, 7 Giro d'Italia stages, 4 Vuelta a España stages and three German national road race titles.

**What did Greipel do with his Tour de France stage bonuses?**

He shared the full bonus with the team that delivered him to the line, every time. The bonus money was, in his framing, the team's payment for the work that produced the win.

**What is the difference between a kamikaze and a gentleman sprinter?**

A kamikaze commits to a gap that is closing, regardless of risk. A gentleman holds a line others can race around safely. Greipel places Jakobsen and Groenewegen in the first group, himself, Kittel and Cavendish in the second.

**Why did André Greipel leave Lotto?**

After his collarbone-breaking Milan-San Remo crash, the replacement contract was for one third of his previous salary, with Grand Tours and monuments excluded. He turned it down.

**Why does Greipel think modern Grand Tour riders will retire young?**

The professionalisation now demanded across nutrition, sleep, altitude work and training load is not sustainable for fifteen years. Combined with how quickly young riders earn now, he expects a generation of retirements in their early thirties.

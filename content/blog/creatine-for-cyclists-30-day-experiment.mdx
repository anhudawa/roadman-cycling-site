I spent ten years telling cyclists creatine was not for them.

I was wrong.

For most of my racing career — cat one license unbroken since 2010, a few of those years racing the bike full time — I had creatine filed in the same drawer as beetroot juice, ketones, and carb rinses. Fads. Things for guys with shakers in the gym. Not for skinny cyclists trying to optimise for power-to-weight.

Then I had Dr. David Dunne on the podcast. He coaches nutrition for half the WorldTour. And in his list of supplements every cyclist should be taking, creatine sat near the top.

I noticed I had stopped updating my knowledge.

So I ran the experiment on myself. Five grams of creatine monohydrate per day for 30 days. No loading phase. Mixed into my post-session recovery shake. That is it. The cheapest supplement on the shelf, around $15 for a tub that lasts months, and the most researched performance compound in sport.

Here is what 30 days actually did. The good, the bad, and the bit nobody talks about.

## The First Two Weeks Were Boring. Then They Were Not.

Week one, nothing.

Genuinely nothing. No hulk-mode. No tingles. No surge of power on the bike. I half-expected to wake up with bulging quads on day two and got mildly smug when I did not. Then I checked the scales.

By the end of week two I was up 1.5 kg.

That is around three pounds. For context, one of those bidons on your bike is 750 millilitres. I was carrying two extra full bottles around with me on every ride. And the research is clear about what that is. It is water. Creatine pulls fluid into muscle cells, the cells hold it, your training weight goes up. Studies put the typical first-fortnight gain at 1 to 2 kg, almost all of it water retention.

This is the bit that scares cyclists off the idea before they have started. Most of us are trained from juniors onwards to think of weight as the thing to subtract, not add. The second the scale started climbing I caught myself wondering if I was getting slower on the climbs.

I was a little freaked out. Even knowing the science.

## The Day-10 Moment

Day 10. Saturday morning. Hill repeats on the local 10-minute climb out the back.

Up the main drag I felt heavy. Long grind, nothing exciting in the legs. Then you turn right at the top and there is a 30-second kicker to the summit. I normally struggle to hold 800 watts on it.

That day I held between 850 and 900.

Got home, downloaded the file off the Hammerhead, checked it twice. Peak was around 900 in the closing seconds of a 30-second effort I was already deep in the red on. That is my best short-term power in years.

That was the inflection point. Up to that morning the experiment was a curiosity. After it, it was data.

By week four my five-second sprint power was up around 5%. The more useful thing was not the single-effort number, though. It was the repeatability. I could string more 10-second efforts together before the time-to-exhaustion drop-off kicked in.

On 20-minute climbing efforts, no change. Heart rate the same. Perceived exertion the same. If anything, a touch slower on the longer steady stuff because of the extra water. That matches the research almost exactly.

This is the headline. Creatine is a turbo for the top end. It is not endurance.

## The Mechanism, Quickly

You do not need a lab to understand why.

Your muscles use ATP for contraction. ATP is the battery. For really short, really hard efforts — sprints, attacks, surges over a climb — the energy system that does the rebuilding is the ATP-PC system. The PC stands for phosphocreatine, which sits in the muscle and recycles ATP fast.

Your natural phosphocreatine stores last about 10 to 15 seconds at maximum effort.

Supplementing increases the size of that reservoir. So you can hold higher power slightly longer before the system runs dry. That is the entire trick. It is not a stimulant. It is not a hormone. It is not new muscle. It is a bigger battery for the efforts that drain the battery in seconds.

There are second-order benefits — improved glycogen storage when paired with carbs, possibly less inflammation after hard sessions, some thermoregulation help in the heat — but the headline mechanism is the punch.

## The Myths I Used to Believe

I held a few of these myself for years. Worth clearing out.

**"Creatine dehydrates you and gives you cramps."** No. The opposite, actually. Holding more water inside muscle cells helps thermoregulation. Studies on soldiers and athletes in heat have found fewer heat-related issues, not more. I trained through a warm patch in week three with no cramping at all. Hydration strategy still matters. Creatine is not drying you out.

**"Creatine wrecks your kidneys."** The data does not support this for healthy individuals on normal doses. The myth comes from creatinine — a waste product whose levels rise when you supplement. That looks alarming on a bloodwork printout if you do not know what you are reading. It is not the same as kidney damage. If you have pre-existing kidney issues, talk to a doctor before adding any supplement.

**"Creatine is basically a steroid."** It is not. No structural relationship to testosterone or any other hormone. WADA-legal at every level of the sport, from local crit to Olympic. The bad reputation seems to come from Soviet labs in the 1950s and 60s researching it heavily during the same period they were producing actual anabolic steroids. Guilt by association.

**"You have to load 20 grams a day for a week."** You do not. I skipped the loading phase entirely. Five grams a day, every day, gets your muscles fully saturated in three to four weeks. The end point is the same. Skipping the load also avoids the GI issues a lot of riders get on a 20-gram protocol.

**"You have to cycle on and off it."** Also no. Creatine is not caffeine. Your receptors do not downregulate. Some riders cycle off in the run-up to a hilly stage race or a hill climb championship to drop the water weight. That is a tactical choice, not a physiological need.

## Match the Tool to the Job

Almost every cyclist will get something out of creatine. The magnitude of the benefit depends on what kind of riding you do.

**Where it earns its keep.** Track sprinters. Crit racers. Anyone whose racing involves repeated surges, breakaways, or sprint finishes. Masters athletes trying to hold on to the muscle they had a decade ago — creatine helps offset age-related strength loss, which matters more every year you stay in the sport. Vegetarians and vegans who start with lower baseline creatine stores often see a bigger jump than meat-eaters do.

**Where the maths gets harder.** Pure climbers. Ultra-endurance riders chasing transcontinental finishes. Anyone where every gram up the hill matters and the efforts are steady-state. The 1.5 kg of water weight matters more than the sprint-power gain. For an event like Badlands with 16,000 metres of climbing, the weight cost is a real conversation.

**Where it is a clear yes.** If you are doing a strength block over winter — in the gym or on the bike doing low-cadence efforts — creatine is a useful pairing. The strength side is where the supplement was originally validated, and you are not racing on water-weighted legs in February.

## What to Do With This

If you decide to try it, keep it simple.

Five grams of creatine monohydrate, every day. Look for a brand that is batch-tested or NSF-certified — the cross-contamination risk in supplement manufacturing is the only reason I would pay attention to brand at all. Mix it into a recovery shake or a glass of water. The timing is not precious. Studies show a marginal edge taking it with carbs post-session, which is what I did, but consistency beats timing.

Tell your coach. Tell your training partner. Log the change in [TrainingPeaks](https://www.trainingpeaks.com) or whatever platform you use so you can actually see what changed. Weight, sprint power, perceived exertion on long climbs — write it down for 30 days.

Stay properly hydrated. Do not expect anything in week one. Expect the scale to climb in week two. Expect the punchy efforts to feel different around week three.

If you race weight-sensitive events, build in an off-period before the key race so you shed the water before the gun goes off.

That is it. There is no protocol secret. The supplement does the work.

## The Honest Verdict

I came into this expecting to confirm what I had told athletes for years. I came out the other side admitting I had been working off old assumptions.

The win was not dramatic. I did not turn into a different rider. I turned into a slightly punchier version of the same rider I already was, with 1.5 kg of water on board. For the kind of racing I do at this stage of my career, that is a fair trade. For the older athletes I coach, the trade gets even better — strength and muscle preservation matter more as the years stack up.

If you have been carrying the same outdated mental model I had, this is one of the easier and cheaper experiments to run on yourself.

Just do it properly. 30 days. One scoop. Log the data. See what your numbers say.

That is the point of an experiment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much creatine should a cyclist take?** Five grams of creatine monohydrate per day, taken consistently. No loading phase needed. Full muscle saturation arrives in three to four weeks at that dose, with fewer GI side effects than a 20-gram loading protocol.

**Will creatine make me heavier on the climbs?** Yes, by 1 to 2 kg in the first fortnight. The weight is almost entirely water held inside muscle cells. Steady climbing power stays the same in absolute terms but slightly worse on a watts-per-kilo basis. Pure climbers and weight-sensitive event riders should time creatine use to off-season blocks.

**Do I need to do a loading phase?** No. Skipping the load gets you to the same end point in three to four weeks with fewer GI side effects and a more gradual water-weight increase.

**Is creatine legal in road racing?** Yes. WADA-permitted at every level of competitive sport. Choose batch-tested or NSF-certified brands to avoid cross-contamination from poorly manufactured supplements.

**Will creatine improve my FTP or 20-minute power?** No. Creatine does not improve aerobic output. The benefit is on efforts under about 30 seconds and on the repeatability of repeated short efforts. Steady-state climbing and time-trial work see no direct lift.

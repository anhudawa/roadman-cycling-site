Most of the people who listen to the Roadman podcast are training between six and ten hours a week. They have a job. They have a family. They have a spouse who would notice if they disappeared on a six-hour ride every Saturday. Almost all of the training advice on the cycling internet is built for athletes with three times their hours. Translating it down to a real working week is half the job.

These are the ten episodes I would queue up for a time-crunched cyclist. Curated, in order, with the takeaway from each.

## Start here

If you only listen to one episode, start with **[Train Slower, Ride Faster — Why It Actually Works](/podcast/ep-2027-train-slower-ride-faster-why-it-actually-works)**. It is the cleanest entry point for a time-crunched rider because it explains why the default amateur instinct — make every ride hard because you only have six hours — actively kills fitness.

The takeaway: the discipline of slowing down on easy days is what makes hard days actually hard. Time-crunched athletes pay the highest cost when they get this wrong.

## The ten episodes, in order

**1. [Train Slower, Ride Faster — Why It Actually Works](/podcast/ep-2027-train-slower-ride-faster-why-it-actually-works)**
Guest: Anthony breaks down the science.
Takeaway: the cost of grey-zone riding is highest when total volume is lowest. Six hours a week run as 50/50 grey-zone produces almost no adaptation. Six hours run as 80/20 produces real fitness.

**2. [I Cut My Training in Half — Here's What No One Tells You](/podcast/ep-2032-i-cut-my-training-in-half-here-s-what-no-one-tells-you)**
Guest: Anthony tracks his own data.
Takeaway: the practical case study. What happened to power, body composition, and recovery when training hours dropped sharply. Spoiler: less than most riders would expect.

Format: vlog.
Takeaway: the structural side. How to find an extra two to three hours of training in a week that already feels full, without adding to total time-on-bike.

## Why these ten

Three patterns run through every episode.

The first is that time-crunched riders pay the highest cost for grey-zone training. A pro riding 30 hours a week can absorb a couple of medium-hard rides without consequence. A working amateur on six hours cannot. The intensity discipline matters more, not less, when total volume is small.

The second is that the high-yield sessions are not exotic. The 4×8 at threshold, the 4×4 torque interval on a climb, the long-ish Zone 2 ride at the weekend. These appear in pro programmes and they appear in well-structured time-crunched programmes. The exotic sessions amateurs chase are rarely worth the recovery cost.

The third is that finding more training time is mostly a structural problem, not a motivational one. The Roadman vlog episode on creating extra training time covers this — small adjustments to commute, family logistics, and weekly planning produce more usable hours than trying harder ever does.

## What the episodes won't tell you

Two things to keep in mind as you listen.

First, polarised training is contested for time-crunched athletes. [Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler) himself is willing to say so. Below six hours a week, a strict 80/20 split often leaves the intensity dose too small to drive adaptation. A more pyramidal distribution — more sweet spot, less pure easy — usually fits better. The [polarised vs sweet spot training](/blog/polarised-vs-sweet-spot-training) comparison goes deeper.

Second, strength training matters more, not less, for time-crunched riders. The temptation when hours are tight is to drop strength because it is not "on the bike." That is the wrong cut. Two strength sessions a week pay back disproportionately in cycling economy and injury resistance, and they take less than two hours of total time. The [strength training course](https://www.roadmancycling.com) covers the structured version.

Pros and amateurs train differently. That is obvious. The more interesting question is what the pros think the amateurs are getting wrong. Coaches will give you their view from the outside. Pros give you the view from inside the system — what they actually do, what they tried as amateurs, and what they wish someone had told them earlier.

This page collects what World Tour and ex-World Tour pros have said specifically about amateur training across their Roadman Cycling Podcast appearances. Five themes recur. Each one is attached to the pros who have stated it most clearly.

## Theme 1 — Amateurs do not eat enough on the bike

This is the single most-repeated observation across pro guests. [Ben Healy](/guests/ben-healy) covered it in detail on his [insane fuelling strategy episode](/podcast/ep-2031-ben-healy-s-insane-fueling-strategy-revealed). His framing: even he was surprised by how much carbohydrate he was eating once he scaled up to where the team nutritionists wanted him. 90 to 120 grams per hour on hard days. He could not have got through a Tour stage on amateur fuelling.

[Michael Matthews](/guests/michael-matthews), who has been in the peloton for 15+ years, has made the same point in his Roadman appearance. The thing amateurs most underestimate about pro life is how much food the job requires. Not how much pain. Not how much volume. How much eating.

The takeaway is consistent. The pros are not lean because they eat less. They are lean because they fuel their training adequately and let body composition follow. Amateurs invert this — they restrict food, ride hard on inadequate fuel, hit the wall, then convince themselves it is a willpower problem.

**What to change:** Build in-ride fuelling deliberately. Start at 60g/hr on hard sessions, work up to 90g/hr over weeks. Use multiple transportable carbs. Use the [fuelling calculator](/tools/fuelling) to plan it.

## Theme 2 — Amateurs ride too hard on easy days

The second-most-repeated observation, and the one that comes up across pros and coaches almost identically. [Lachlan Morton](/guests/lachlan-morton) talked about this on his [why I quit World Tour episode](/podcast/ep-21-my-untold-story-about-why-i-quit-world-tour-lachlan-morton) — not as a critique of amateurs, but as a description of what it took for him to learn to genuinely back off on easy days as a young pro. The instinct to push runs deep. The discipline to control intensity is what separates the pros who keep improving from those who burn out.

[Fred Wright](/guests/fred-wright) made a related point on his [too much training episode](/podcast/ep-2164-ride-your-bike-too-much-and-this-happens-fred-wright-rdmn-cl). The mistake is treating fatigue as progress. Pros do not. Pros treat fatigue as a signal that adaptation has not yet been allowed to happen.

The grey zone — the moderate-effort range that feels productive because it is uncomfortable but produces flat fitness because the body cannot recover from it — is the default mistake of self-coached amateurs. It is what [Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler) has built a career on naming, and it is what the pros confirm from their side.

**What to change:** Audit your last four weeks. If your easy rides drift above LT1, slow them down even when you feel good. The discipline is the work.

## Theme 3 — Amateurs over-rate FTP relative to durability

[Dan Bigham](/guests/dan-bigham), the former UCI Hour Record holder and Head of Engineering at Red Bull-Bora-Hansgrohe, has been the most direct on this on the podcast. His [aerodynamics episode](/podcast/ep-2106-he-accidentally-mastered-aerodynamics-dan-bigham) is partly about wind tunnels and partly about how amateurs misuse FTP as a complete picture of fitness.

His framing: a single 20-minute or ramp-test FTP value tells you what the rider can produce in one specific test condition. It tells you very little about what they can produce after three hours of riding, or after a hard interval block, or in the wind. Pros are tested implicitly across thousands of training files. Amateurs run a single test and treat the result as a verdict.

The deeper issue is that durability — the ability to hold meaningful power deep into a long ride — is the metric that separates fast amateurs from genuinely competitive ones, and durability is built by the long aerobic ride that most amateurs cut from their week to make room for more intervals.

**What to change:** Reduce the weight you put on any single FTP test. Use the [FTP zone calculator](/tools/ftp-zones) for zones, then track durability separately. A one-hour FTP test result that does not survive a three-hour ride is a number, not fitness.

## Theme 4 — Amateurs underestimate sleep and stress

This is the theme pros raise that amateurs most often dismiss. [Greg LeMond](/guests/greg-lemond) talked about it on his Roadman appearances, and so have multiple younger pros across rider-support episodes. The recovery side of the ledger is treated as primary by pros. By amateurs, it is often treated as a secondary variable that can be optimised after the training is sorted.

The pros' framing is structural: cortisol does not distinguish between training stress and life stress. A working amateur with chronic sleep restriction below six hours, a high-pressure job, and a long commute is producing a recovery environment that no training programme can override. The training prescription is upstream of the recovery environment, not separate from it.

**What to change:** Track sleep for two weeks. Protect the bottom of your range first — reducing the worst nights produces more performance change than chasing the best ones. Treat stress as a training variable.

## Theme 5 — Amateurs skip the unsexy fundamentals

This is the cumulative theme that pulls the others together. The pros who have spoken about amateur training on Roadman do not point at exotic sessions or proprietary protocols. They point at the unsexy fundamentals: the long ride, strength training, fuelling, sleep, recovery, intensity discipline.

[Michael Matthews](/guests/michael-matthews) made a version of this point on his Roadman episode — the gap between what pros do and what amateurs picture pros doing is mostly about consistency, not about secret sessions. [David Millar](/guests/david-millar) has made similar observations on his appearances.

The pros are doing the same things, executed with much higher consistency over much longer time horizons. The amateur instinct is to look for the missing ingredient. There is no missing ingredient. There is just the daily, weekly, yearly compounding of getting the unsexy parts right.

**What to change:** Stop looking for the missing session. Audit how well you are executing the boring stuff. Long ride every week. Two strength sessions. Fuelled hard sessions. Genuine easy days. Adequate sleep. The unglamorous list is the list.

## What pros do not say

Worth noting what the pros on Roadman do not say about amateur training, because the silence is informative.

They do not say amateurs need more volume. None of the pro guests have framed amateur underperformance as primarily a volume problem. The framing is execution quality, not training quantity.

They do not say amateurs need exotic sessions. The sessions in pro programmes are the same sessions amateurs already know — 4×8 at threshold, long aerobic rides, torque intervals on climbs. The difference is in execution and consistency.

They do not say amateurs need to copy pro training. Several pros — Lorang from the coaching side, Bigham from the engineering side — have been explicit that pro training does not transfer cleanly to amateur volume and recovery capacity. The principles transfer. The numbers do not.

## Coaching

If you have read this and recognised yourself in three or more of the themes, that is what coaching is for. The [Roadman coaching programme](/coaching) is built around the unsexy fundamentals these pros keep pointing at — fuelling, intensity discipline, the long ride, strength, recovery — sequenced and adjusted to your real life. Not Done Yet is the paid community at the centre of it.

Start with an [assessment](/assessment) if you want a coach to read your training the way these pros would and tell you which two of the five themes are costing you the most. The answer is usually fuelling and intensity discipline, in that order. Closing those two gaps is where most of the change comes from.

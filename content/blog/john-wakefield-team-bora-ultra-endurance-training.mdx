Submaximal fatigue test every 7 to 10 days. Three minutes at threshold. A short questionnaire after.

That is how John Wakefield knows whether your training is working.

Not after a six-week block. Not at the end of a phase. Every week and a bit.

[Listen to the full conversation on the Roadman Cycling Podcast →](/podcast/ep-2132-how-do-team-bora-approach-building-endurance-we-find-out-joh)

I sat down with John Wakefield for the better part of an hour. Head coach at WorldTour team Bora-Hansgrohe. Hands-on with Primož Roglič and Jai Hindley. Came up through Science to Sport in South Africa where he turned Matt Beers and Ashleigh Moolman Pasio into hitters. Now also building plans for some of the biggest ultra-distance amateur athletes in the world — Badlands, Unbound, Traka.

The brief was specific. Forget the WorldTour for a minute. If a rider has six to eight hours a week and a goal of finishing one of these monster gravel events, what is the actual plan?

## The Test Most Amateurs Have Never Heard Of

Here is the thing nobody tells you about how WorldTour teams monitor fatigue. They are not waiting for you to crack on a Saturday and message the coach on Sunday. They are running short, repeatable, in-ride tests every 7 to 10 days that pick up the problem before you feel it.

Wakefield calls it a submaximal fatigue test. It is a refined version of the Lambert protocol he and his business partner Jen Sweet brought into UAE Team Emirates and now run at Bora.

The protocol is simple. Three minutes at a fixed power somewhere between threshold and 110% of threshold, performed in the first 10 minutes of whatever ride is already on your plan. Then a short questionnaire — RPE, time-to-exertion feel, sleep, mood, wellness, bodyweight.

Wakefield correlates the lot. If your stimulus and your recovery are aligned, the numbers move in the right direction. If you are over-cooked, they move the other way. If you have plateaued, they sit flat. *"When done correctly, reporting from the athlete, it's almost bulletproof."*

The bit I want every amateur to take seriously is the frequency. You cannot wait six weeks to find out you have been digging a hole. You need a feedback loop short enough to course-correct inside a single block. Seven to 10 days is that loop. Most age-group plans do not have one.

## How He Periodises An Ultra Build

When I asked Wakefield to walk through the structure of a Badlands or Unbound build, the answer was almost reassuringly traditional. Three weeks on, one week off. Or four on, one off. Whichever the athlete can actually absorb.

Base into preparation into medium intensity into high intensity into taper. He plays with the order of some blocks, but reverse periodisation is not his default for an ultra build. Get the foundation in. Layer the specific work on top. Taper into the event.

The detail that matters is not the block shape. It is what goes inside it. For ultra events specifically, Wakefield pushes two things most amateur plans skim over. Off-bike strength. And on-bike neuromuscular work.

## Why Torque Beats Cadence At Hour Thirty

Watch the cadence data from anyone finishing Badlands or a 500km ultra. The number drops as the hours pass. By the back end of a 780 kilometre event, you are not turning over a high cadence anymore. You are grinding through torque.

Wakefield's words. *"There will be a point where you're not running on watts, you're running on newton-metres of torque. When that fatigue kicks in, you need to revert to torque to keep going."*

His fix is to train it specifically. From day one with a new athlete, not as a peak-phase add-on. Low-cadence strength on the bike — what Alan Davis used to call "old school strengthies" — paired with proper gym work off the bike.

The why is simple. If your only mode of forward propulsion is a high cadence at moderate force, the moment that cadence drops you are out of options. Train the torque end of the curve and you have somewhere to go when the legs stop spinning.

And yes — he still includes sprint and VO2 work for ultra athletes. The reasoning is straightforward. Raise the ceiling and everything underneath moves up with it. Just because you do not sprint in the event does not mean you skip sprints in training.

## Sustainable Power Beats Aggressive Aero

We got onto Dylan Johnson's argument that slower riders should care more about aero, because they are out in the wind for longer. Wakefield does not disagree with the maths. He disagrees with the application.

His position is direct. An aggressive aero position is only worth anything if you can hold it. If you are forced out of your tuck after hour four because your back is screaming, you have given back every watt of saving and added muscular cost on top.

He puts it bluntly. A rider who can hold 220 watts in a comfortable position for the full event will beat a rider who can only hold an aggressive position until fatigue forces him upright.

This is the part most amateurs miss when they kit themselves out for a target gravel race. The fit that wins is not the fit that looks fastest in the wind tunnel. It is the fit that still works at hour 20.

## The Less-Is-More Truth For Serious Amateurs

The most important thing Wakefield said in the whole conversation was about what happens to the most committed amateurs. Not the lazy ones. The ones who pour everything into it.

These are the riders with the budget. The kit. The brain. The free weekends. They read the studies. They run the metrics. They train harder than the pros they idolise. And they get worse.

Wakefield's actual words. *"Some of them are doing more than what a full-time professional landing on the podium at Traka is doing... maybe do less, bring the fun back into it, and you'll actually have a better reward."*

The reason needs spelling out. Pros have one job — they train, recover, sleep, eat, ride. The stress load is contained. You have a 9-to-5, a family, a commute, a mortgage, a relationship, a business. The bandwidth left for training stress is a fraction of theirs. Layer pro-level training volume on top of all of that and the system breaks. Not dramatically. Quietly. The fatigue test starts trending the wrong way. The legs sit on the threshold of "off" for weeks.

Wakefield's prescription is the one most coaches will not say out loud. Some weeks, do less. Bring the fun back. Stop digging.

## Rest Is The Underrated Lever

Toward the end of the episode I asked the question every amateur is curious about. Compression boots. Ketones. Ice baths. Massage. What is Bora actually using that the rest of us should copy?

His answer was a single word. Rest.

Most amateurs are not under-recovered because they cannot afford a Theragun. They are under-recovered because they are too scared to take a day off. They feel slow on Wednesday so they ride harder on Thursday. The 4x4 VO2 set becomes 6x4. The hole gets deeper.

Wakefield's framework is the one most riders need to hear. As an amateur you cannot easily move the frequency lever — only so many days are available. You cannot easily move the duration lever — only so many hours fit in a week. So when fatigue lands, the only lever left is intensity, and you yank it the wrong way.

The fix is to take one step back to go two forward. Rest. Let the work absorb. Let the test catch up. Then build again.

## What Amateur Cyclists Can Actually Take From This

You are not riding for Bora. The principles still translate.

**1. Build a fatigue feedback loop.** A weekly three-minute submaximal test before a ride is not WorldTour-only. Use it. Track RPE, sleep, mood, bodyweight against a fixed power. The numbers will tell you what your legs already know.

**2. Train the torque end of the curve.** Off-bike strength plus low-cadence on-bike work is non-negotiable for ultra goals. The moment your cadence drops, torque is the only currency left.

**3. Sustainable power wins ultras.** A position you can hold all day beats a tuck you abandon at hour four. Fit for the duration, not for the wind tunnel.

**4. Less is sometimes the answer.** If you have the resources, the brain, and the discipline of a full-time pro but the life of an amateur, the bigger lever is often subtraction. Do less. Recover better. Watch the test results.

**5. Discipline travels.** Wakefield's most-professional athletes — Alexander Kristoff among them — all share one trait. Attention to detail. Bag packed. Bike clean. Nutrition prepped. The most copyable thing pros do is also the cheapest.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How does John Wakefield monitor fatigue with WorldTour riders?**

A submaximal fatigue test every 7 to 10 days. The athlete performs a three-minute effort at a fixed power between threshold and 110% of threshold in the first 10 minutes of any planned ride, then completes a short questionnaire on RPE, sleep, mood, wellness and bodyweight. The trend tells him whether the stimulus is right, whether the athlete is over-fatigued, or whether progress has stalled.

**What kind of periodisation does he use for ultra-endurance events?**

Traditional 3+1 or 4+1 blocks — three or four weeks of progressive load followed by a recovery week — depending on what the athlete can absorb. The structure runs base, preparation, medium intensity, high intensity, taper. Reverse periodisation is not his default for an ultra build.

**Why does torque training matter for ultra-distance gravel events?**

Cadence drops as fatigue accumulates. By the back end of a 780 kilometre event like Badlands, riders cannot maintain a high cadence — they grind through torque. Wakefield trains low-cadence strength on the bike and gym work off the bike from the start of an ultra build, so riders have a second mode of propulsion when their cadence collapses.

158 professional wins. 11 Tour de France stages. Seven at the Giro. Four at the Vuelta.

And he gave every Tour stage bonus away. Split it with the whole team. Every single time.

That tells you most of what you need to know about [André Greipel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Greipel).

[Listen to the full conversation on the Roadman Cycling Podcast →](/podcast/ep-2240-what-makes-a-sprinter-unbeatable-andr-greipel)

I sat down with André after he closed the door on a career most riders would still be chasing. The Lotto years — the bunch sprints he was supposed to lose, the years where if Greipel got a clean run to 250 metres out the rest were racing for second. Eight Tours. Eleven Tour stages. The German Gorilla.

The story behind the man, though, is not the wattage. It is what he was actually carrying.

## The Bonus Nobody Talks About

Pro contracts have win bonuses written into them. Especially Tour stage bonuses. They are not small. And every sprinter you can name, with a contract that includes them, is the rider who hits the line first.

Greipel never took his.

Not once. Not in a Lotto year. Not when he won three stages in one race. Not when he was the reason eight other riders rode through the wind for 200 kilometres. Every Tour bonus, split with the team that delivered him there.

The reasoning is simple if you have ever sat in a sprint train. The lead-out is not a service. It is a partnership. The riders pulling on the front into a headwind are not employees. They are the reason the win is possible. And if you are the only one in the car park with a bonus written into your contract, you have already broken the maths of the team.

Sharing the bonus, in his own words, was a tool to motivate the team. He earned good money. He wanted his team to earn good money too. Same race, same risk — same payday.

This is the bit the YouTube highlights cannot show you. The sprint captain is not just the fastest rider on the team. He is the rider responsible for the financial and emotional state of the seven other riders who put their season into a 12-second window. Greipel got that. Most don't.

## Kamikaze Sprinters vs Gentleman Sprinters

There is a phrase Rick Zabel uses that I asked André about on the podcast.

There are two types of sprinters. The kamikaze sprinter goes for any gap, regardless of who is in it. The gentleman sprinter wants to win, but not at the cost of putting another rider in hospital.

André agrees with the framing. He puts Fabio Jakobsen, Dylan Groenewegen and Phil Bauhaus in the kamikaze category. He puts himself in the other one. Plenty of riders teased him over the years for being too friendly on the bike. Not enough elbows. His response is a number — 158 races. You don't get to that figure without elbows. Just not the kind you have to apologise for in the press conference.

The wider point is the one nobody in the cycling press will tell you straight. He came up at a time when Cipollini and Petacchi were still racing. If a young sprinter did something stupid on a finishing straight, those guys would tell you. With words first. With a wheel into the barrier the next time, if the words didn't work. The respect figures are mostly gone now. The young guys want to prove themselves and there is no Cipollini sitting on their wheel reminding them of the unwritten rules.

The other point is sharper. Some of these crashes are race organiser problems, not rider problems. The downhill sprint into Katowice at the Tour de Pologne hits 85 to 90 km/h. André raced that finish multiple times and never sprinted. Crossed the line, went straight to the commissaires, told them the same thing every year. *That sprint is insane. You can't do this. This is just not normal.*

The crash that put Jakobsen in a coma did not need to happen. The course did not need to be there. Punishing rider behaviour is reasonable. Not putting a sprint at 90 km/h on a downhill in the first place is the bigger fix.

## Why the Young Generation Won't Race At 38

I asked him about Pogacar and Remco. The 23-year-olds with Tour wins, World titles, Monuments before they have left the under-23 bracket.

He doesn't think any of them will be racing at 38.

Not because they aren't talented enough. Because the modern WorldTour does not allow it.

His phrase was that cycling has changed in the last six or seven years. Sleep correct. Eat correct. Altitude camps. Train at 100% every day. He is not against any of it — he did the same. But he is realistic about what that costs. Eventually the body shuts down. And if it doesn't, the head does. He named Tom Dumoulin without me having to mention him. The career that was supposed to roll on for another five years ended early because the head said no.

The frame I keep coming back to here is that André is not romanticising the old days. He is just saying the bill comes due. And the riders coming up now are signing for the same bill, only earlier.

For the amateur reading this — and yes, this applies to you — the principle is the same. Stress is stress. Training stress is just one type of it. If the rest of your life is also operating at 100%, the bike is not the place where it stays clean. The riders who last are not the ones who hit hardest. They are the ones who manage the stress environment around the work.

The fixable part is not always the training plan. It is sometimes how much room you give the rest of the week.

## The Lotto Exit Nobody Saw Coming

If you watched cycling in the 2010s, you remember the Lotto Greipel years. The red kit, the hands in the air, the bunch sprints he was supposed to lose finishing with him still on the front. He would have ended his career there. He told me that. He wanted to.

What actually happened is one of the more brutal stories I have heard on the podcast.

In 2018 he made it over the Poggio at Milan-San Remo for the first time in his career. Then he crashed on the descent. Broke his collarbone. The same evening, management told him to come in once he was fit and they would sort the new contract.

The offer never came.

He came back twelve weeks later. Won two stages at the Tour of Denmark. Finished second overall by one second. Two days before the Tour de France started, the contract offer finally arrived.

One third of his salary. No grand tours. No Monuments.

He turned it down. By his own description he had just saved their season. The lead-out riders were not being offered contracts either. The team that had been built around him was being dismantled before his eyes.

He left.

Why does this matter for the audience? Because the cycling industry, even at the very top, is not sentimental. The same rider who made the highlight reel for eight years gets a one-third offer and a Grand Tour ban two days before the biggest race of the year. There is no ceremony. There is no thanks. The sport moves on the moment it thinks you have.

That cuts both ways for the amateur as well. The version of you that the local club tipped to a category up last year is not who they remember if you stop showing up at the Saturday hammerfest for six months. You set the tone. The bunch follows it. André had to do the same — leave on his terms rather than wait to be pushed.

## What Amateur Riders Can Actually Take From This

You are not winning a Tour stage. Neither am I. The principles are not just for the WorldTour.

**1. Your team is the result.** If you race at any level, the riders around you are the reason your win is possible. Treat them that way. Whether that is buying coffee for the lead-out at the local crit, or actually rotating turns properly on the Saturday bunch ride, the unit only works if everyone knows they are valued. The riders we work with on the [Roadman coaching system](/coaching) compound results faster when the social side is solid, not just the watts.

**2. Choose the right type of sprinter to be.** You do not need to be the kamikaze. The riders you respect at the top of the sport — Greipel, Cavendish on a good day, Sagan — won by repeated, clean, clinical positioning. Not by going for the gap that ends the season for the rider in front of them. This applies at club level too. The reputation you build comes home with you.

**3. Manage the stress, not just the training.** This is the line André kept pulling back to. The body shuts down when the head shuts down. If you are running on fumes at work, with a kid not sleeping, and trying to add a fourth interval session, something is going to give. Recovery is not a separate budget. It is the same budget.

**4. Know when to leave.** This is the unspoken one. André walked away because his head was no longer in it. Most riders ride past that signal for a year or two. Some pay for it longer than that. The willingness to step back from a goal — for a season, for a winter, for whatever you need — is what allows you to come back. The "Not Done Yet" mindset is not 100% all the time. It is knowing what to push and what to let go.

**5. Helmets matter more than the marketing tells you.** This was a small part of the conversation but worth pulling out. André now works with UVEX on the safety side of helmet design. He is honest about the fact that consumer helmets are still mostly marketed on aero and style. Safety is the boring part nobody puts in the ad. If you are riding in a fast group, ask yourself if your helmet is the right one for the speeds you are actually doing — not the one that looked good in the photo.

Cycling nutrition is the area where bad advice has done the most damage to the most riders. The "calories in versus calories out" framing has produced a generation of cyclists who under-fuel their training, restrict during the day, then bonk at hour three of the weekend long ride and convince themselves they have a willpower problem. They do not. They have a fuelling problem.

These are the twelve episodes I would queue up for a cyclist who wants to actually fix nutrition — fuel performance properly and let body composition follow. Curated, in order, with the takeaway from each.

## Start here

If you only listen to one episode, start with **[World Tour Nutritionist: We Got Weight Loss Wrong](/podcast/ep-2044-world-tour-nutritionist-we-got-weight-loss-wrong)**. It is the cleanest demolition of the calorie-restriction model in the archive. The takeaway: chronic under-fuelling does not produce sustainable weight loss. It produces performance loss with weight loss as a temporary side effect, followed by the weight returning when the rider rebounds.

## Why these twelve

Three patterns run through every episode in the list.

The first is that pros eat dramatically more carbohydrate than amateurs assume — both during rides and across the day. The image of the lean pro who barely eats is wrong. The lean pros eat to fuel their training and recover from it, and the leanness is a downstream consequence, not the goal.

The second is that body composition is achieved through performance fuelling, not restriction. Anthony's own 7 kg loss while eating more, Alex Larson's framework, and the World Tour nutritionist episodes all point at the same conclusion. The riders who hold body composition long-term are the ones who fuel training adequately. Restriction works briefly and then breaks.

The third is that the in-ride numbers matter. 60 to 90 grams of carbohydrate per hour for hard sessions is the modern evidence-based range, with the upper bound (90–120 g/hr) accessible to athletes who train the gut. Most amateurs are at 30 to 40. Closing that gap is the single fastest way to improve hard-session quality and the adaptations that follow.

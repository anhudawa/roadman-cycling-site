There are plenty of "training mistakes" lists on the cycling internet. Most are synthesised from secondary sources — what someone read someone else said. This one is different. Every mistake on this page is one that a named coach or sports scientist has explicitly called out on the Roadman Cycling Podcast, in their own framing.

Eight entries. The coach who flagged each one. The episode they said it on. And the practical fix. If a working World Tour coach has named it, and your training has it, that is the place to start.

## 1. Riding easy days too hard — Stephen Seiler

The mistake [Prof. Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler) names more often than any other on the podcast. Easy days that drift above the first lactate threshold (LT1) — the pace where you can hold a complete sentence without pausing for breath — are the mechanical reason most amateurs do not improve. Too hard to allow recovery. Too easy to drive top-end adaptation.

Seiler's framing on the [80/20 training episode](/podcast/ep-2148-80-20-training-to-ride-faster-dr-stephen-seiler) was that elite athletes do not do more hard sessions than recreational riders. They do more easy sessions. The 80% volume floor below LT1 is what makes the 20% above LT2 actually land.

**Fix:** Audit your last four weeks. Count rides that were genuinely below LT1 versus rides that crept into the grey zone. Most amateurs find the ratio is 50/50, not 80/20. Slow down on easy days even when you feel good — that is the discipline.

## 2. Under-fuelling hard sessions — Dan Lorang and John Wakefield

Both [Dan Lorang](/guests/dan-lorang) and [John Wakefield](/guests/john-wakefield) have named this on the podcast. Lorang on his [amateur training plan episode](/podcast/ep-2134-roglics-coach-builds-a-training-plan-for-amateur-riders-dan). Wakefield on the [Bora endurance episode](/podcast/ep-2132-how-do-team-bora-approach-building-endurance-we-find-out-joh).

Their framing: hard sessions in a glycogen-depleted state are not training. They are completing a workout while leaving the adaptation on the floor. World Tour pros target 90 to 120 grams of carbohydrate per hour during hard sessions using multiple transportable carbs. Most amateurs ride at 30 to 40. The session quality collapses, the training stimulus is blunted, and the rider walks away tired without the adaptation they were chasing.

**Fix:** Build fuelling deliberately. Start at 60g/hr, work up to 90g/hr over weeks. Use multiple transportable carbs (glucose plus fructose, roughly 2:1). Track it as carefully as power. Pair this with the [fuelling calculator](/tools/fuelling) for session-level planning.

## 3. Skipping strength training — Joe Friel and Dan Lorang

[Joe Friel](/guests/joe-friel) has been the loudest voice on this for over a decade. On his [ideal training week episode](/podcast/ep-40-how-joe-friel-structures-the-ideal-cycling-training-week), he was direct: strength work is the most neglected tool in the amateur programme, and the gap between what coaches prescribe and what amateurs actually do is widest here.

Lorang's framing is structural. Strength is not a discipline that competes with endurance. It is a tool that protects the body's ability to express endurance. He programmes year-round strength for his cyclists and his triathletes. Two sessions per week. Heavy compound lifts (3–5 reps), single-leg variation, posterior chain emphasis. Not circuit-style cardio.

**Fix:** Two strength sessions a week, year-round. Time them so they do not compromise key bike sessions — most coaches programme strength immediately after a hard ride, on the same day. See [strength training for cyclists](/blog/cycling-strength-training-essentials) for the structured version.

## 4. No periodisation — Joe Friel

Friel named this on his Roadman appearance and it is the central thesis of The Cyclist's Training Bible. The mistake is doing approximately the same training week after week — same sessions, same intensity, same volume. It is the single most common structure among self-coached amateurs and it produces a plateau within six to twelve months, often sooner.

Friel's framing: training without periodisation is "training without sequence." The body adapts to a specific stress only after enough time at that stress to produce a measurable change. Mixing all phases simultaneously is less efficient than developing each in sequence — base, build, peak, race.

**Fix:** Plan in 3+1 or 4+1 blocks. Three to four progressive weeks, one deload. Each block has a defined physiological target. "Train hard" is not a target. "Build aerobic base at 80% sub-threshold volume" is.

## 5. Retesting FTP too often — Dan Bigham

[Dan Bigham](/guests/dan-bigham), the former UCI Hour Record holder, has been particularly direct about this on the podcast. The amateur instinct is to retest FTP every four weeks and treat each test as a verdict. Bigham's view, drawing on his pro and engineering background, is that lab- or test-derived FTP values frequently fail to reflect real-world performance — and the obsession with the number distracts from the training that produces it.

His framing on his episode: most pros do not formally FTP-test the way amateurs do. They read training data continuously. The zones reveal themselves through power files, not through a single dramatic test.

**Fix:** Reduce the weight you put on any single FTP test. Retest only when your training block warrants it, not on a fixed calendar. Use the [FTP zone calculator](/tools/ftp-zones) to set zones from your most recent reliable test, then read your training data on an ongoing basis to confirm calibration.

## 6. Training through fatigue rather than recovering from it — Dan Lorang

Lorang has named this on multiple appearances. His framing on the [13 years of coaching pros episode](/podcast/ep-2056-13-years-of-coaching-pros-what-amateurs-dont-know) was that the athletes who improve fastest are not the ones who train hardest. They are the ones who recover with the same level of intent.

The specific mistake amateurs make is treating fatigue as a sign of progress and recovery as a sign of weakness. The pros do the opposite. Sleep, nutrition timing, training density, and stress load are treated as primary training variables, not secondary ones. Every third or fourth week is a reduced-load week, scheduled before it is needed.

**Fix:** Plan deloads rather than waiting for fatigue to force them. Track sleep for two weeks and protect the bottom of your range first. Treat the recovery side of the ledger with the same discipline you treat the training side. Use the [recovery questions on the assessment](/assessment) to start.

## 7. Trading the long ride for more intervals — Lorang and Wakefield

This is the mistake Lorang has been most blunt about. His framing on the amateur training plan episode: if a working amateur can only do two sessions in a week, one of them should always be the long aerobic ride. Not a hammer-fest. A controlled three-to-five-hour ride that builds the engine every other session depends on.

The amateur logic — short on time, so cut the long ride and replace it with shorter, harder intervals — is exactly the wrong direction. Wakefield said the same in different words on the Bora episode. The long ride drives adaptations that intervals cannot replicate. Skipping it removes the structural foundation the intervals are supposed to be built on.

**Fix:** Block out the long ride first, then schedule everything else around it. Three hours minimum on most weeks. Controlled — Zone 2 by power, conversational by feel, with deliberate fuelling throughout.

## 8. Using AI as a coach — Anthony Walsh, supported by multiple guests

This one is mine, supported by what guests have said on the podcast. The mistake is treating an AI tool as a substitute for a working coach. AI can generate plans. It cannot read the rider. It cannot integrate the conversation about how the last block actually felt, the family stress that hit in week three, the niggle that appeared in week four, or the goal event that just got pushed back two months.

Coaching is a continuous adjustment, not a static plan. The pros who get the most out of their coaches are not getting better plans. They are getting better adjustment. AI does not do adjustment. It produces plausible-looking plans that decay fast against real-world life.

**Fix:** If you are using AI to draft sessions, fine — that is reasonable. If you are using it as a substitute for human feedback on your training, that is the gap. The [Roadman coaching programme](/coaching) is built around human adjustment specifically because no current AI tool can replace it.

## What to do if you recognise more than two

Most riders will recognise three or four of these in their current training. That is normal. The interactions between the mistakes matter as much as the individual mistakes themselves. Fixing fuelling without fixing intensity distribution produces different results than fixing both together.

The pragmatic order: fix intensity distribution first (mistake 1), then fuelling (mistake 2), then the long ride (mistake 7). Those three changes alone produce visible fitness change inside three to four weeks for most riders. Strength and periodisation come next. The ego-laden ones — retesting too often, training through fatigue — usually require an outside voice to fix.

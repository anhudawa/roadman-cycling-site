The cycling internet makes World Tour coaches sound like they hate each other. They do not. Strip away the marketing of individual programmes and the actual day-to-day prescriptions for FTP development across the top of the sport are remarkably consistent. The disagreements are real, but they sit at the implementation layer, not at the level of principle.

This page is the consensus, drawn from multiple Roadman Cycling Podcast appearances by working World Tour coaches — [Dan Lorang](/guests/dan-lorang) at Red Bull-Bora-Hansgrohe, his deputy [John Wakefield](/guests/john-wakefield), references to Tim Kerrison's Team Sky/Ineos approach, and the conversations I have had with the wider network. Five things they agree on, two things they don't, and what each one means for an amateur trying to move the number.

## What they agree on

### 1. Aerobic base sets the FTP ceiling

Every World Tour coach on the Roadman archive returns to the same point: FTP is a lagging indicator of aerobic capacity. The 95 percent of LT2 figure is just the threshold expression of an aerobic system that has been built over months and years. You cannot push FTP up sustainably with intervals if the underlying engine is too small to support them.

Lorang's framing on the [Roglič episode](/podcast/ep-2134-roglics-coach-builds-a-training-plan-for-amateur-riders-dan) was that World Tour riders spend most of the year building or protecting the base. Wakefield, on his Bora-endurance episode, described the same logic at team level. The threshold blocks are short, deliberate, and sit on top of much larger volumes of low-intensity work.

**Application:** Before you programme any FTP-specific block, audit the base. If you cannot ride three to four hours at Zone 2 without fading, the threshold work is going to underdeliver. Eight to twelve weeks of structured base is not optional.

### 2. Threshold work is dosed, not stacked

Pro coaches do not run threshold blocks the way amateurs do. The amateur default is two or three threshold sessions a week for eight weeks, treating threshold as the centrepiece. The pro default is one to two well-designed threshold sessions a week, surrounded by easy aerobic work, run in repeating 3+1 or 4+1 blocks.

This is a point Lorang has made repeatedly. The session quality matters more than the session count. A clean 4×8 at FTP, fuelled properly, recovered into, is worth more than three messy sessions stacked on grey-zone fatigue. Wakefield said the same on the Bora endurance episode — the hard sessions in pro programmes are often surprisingly few, and almost always the same canonical blocks repeated.

**Application:** Cap threshold sessions at two per week during a build phase. One per week during base. Build in a deload every fourth week. Stop chasing more frequency and start protecting the quality of the sessions you have.

### 3. Low cadence and torque work is now standard

Five years ago this was contested. It is not any more. [John Wakefield](/guests/john-wakefield) has talked on the podcast about the torque-interval prescriptions he runs at Red Bull-Bora-Hansgrohe — typically four-minute efforts on a 4–7% gradient at 40–60 RPM, RPE 7/10. Tim Kerrison's old Team Sky programme included similar work. Dan Lorang programmes it for both his cyclists and his triathletes.

The science caught up in 2024. The Habiš and Habiš study published in PLOS ONE showed low cadence interval groups improved VO2max by 8.7 percent versus 4.6 percent for freely chosen cadence, with maximum aerobic power up 8.1 percent versus 3 percent. The earlier studies that found nothing — Christopherson 2014, Nimmer 2012 — used protocols too short, too light, or with wrong cadence ranges to actually test what coaches were prescribing.

**Application:** Add one torque session a week during a base or early-build phase. Wakefield's protocol: 4×4-minute climbs at 40–60 RPM, RPE 7/10, four minutes recovery. Or longer 8–12-minute efforts at 50–65 RPM on flatter terrain. Keep total weekly volume at this cadence range modest — it is a stimulus, not a default.

### 4. Fuelling decides whether a threshold session counts

This is the area where the World Tour coaches sound most identical. Hard sessions in a glycogen-depleted state are not training. They are completing a workout while leaving the adaptation on the floor. Lorang programmes 90–120g/hr of carbohydrate for hard sessions in his pros. Wakefield is in the same range. The 2:1 glucose-fructose ratio is now standard practice.

The number matters because the session quality is downstream of the substrate availability. A rider who hits 95% of FTP for the first three intervals and 82% for the last two is not training what the workout was designed to train. The workout was designed to train sustained threshold output. Half of it actually did.

**Application:** Build fuelling deliberately into the session. Start at 60g/hr, work up to 90g/hr. Use multiple transportable carbs. Track it as carefully as you track power. Most amateurs make this single change and see threshold-session power lift inside two weeks.

### 5. Testing is lighter and more frequent than amateurs make it

The formal FTP test as it exists in amateur training — a single 20-minute or ramp test that defines the next eight weeks of zones — is largely a pro construct that has been bent by amateur use. World Tour coaches have access to power files from every training ride and can read drift in real time. They do not need a single dramatic test to set zones. The zones reveal themselves through training data.

Lorang and Wakefield have both said on the podcast that they prefer repeated, controlled efforts within training — sub-threshold blocks, sustained climbs, race-pace efforts — over a single FTP test. [Dan Bigham](/guests/dan-bigham), the former Hour Record holder, has been even more direct about the limits of lab-derived FTP values when the goal is real-world performance.

**Application:** Reduce the weight you put on any single FTP test. Use the [FTP zone calculator](/tools/ftp-zones) to set zones from your most recent reliable test, then read your training data on an ongoing basis to confirm the zones are still calibrated. If sub-threshold work feels markedly easier or harder than it should, the zones have moved before the next formal test would have caught it.

## Where they actually disagree

### Disagreement 1 — Test protocol

The 20-minute test with 95% multiplier remains common, but several coaches now prefer ramp tests, durability tests (FTP after a long aerobic block), or repeated 8-minute efforts. The choice depends on what the rider's events demand. A criterium racer's actionable FTP looks different to a Grand Tour rider's actionable FTP, and the test should reflect that. There is no universal right answer here, only a right answer for a specific athlete and event.

### Disagreement 2 — Polarised vs sweet spot for time-limited athletes

The biggest live debate. [Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler)'s polarised research suggests an 80/20 split. Lorang agrees broadly but programmes more sweet spot for time-limited athletes. Sweet-spot advocates argue that a 6-hour-a-week rider cannot afford the easy volume that makes pure polarised effective. Polarised advocates argue that sweet spot easily collapses into grey zone without strict discipline.

Both positions are defensible. The pragmatic answer is that the rider's total weekly hours should determine the distribution. Above 10 hours, polarised. Below 6, more pyramidal. The 6–10-hour band is genuinely contested. See [polarised vs sweet spot training](/blog/polarised-vs-sweet-spot-training) for the working comparison.

## What this means for your next FTP block

Five questions before you start an eight-to-twelve week FTP block:

1. Is your aerobic base sufficient — can you ride three to four hours at Zone 2 without fading?
2. Are you capping threshold sessions at one to two per week with a deload every fourth?
3. Have you got at least one torque or low-cadence session in the weekly plan?
4. Are you fuelling hard sessions at 60–90g/hr and tracking it as carefully as power?
5. Is your zone setting based on a recent reliable test rather than an old number that has drifted?

If the answers are yes, you have built the conditions World Tour coaches actually agree on. The block will move the number. If two or more answers are no, the gap is upstream of FTP itself.

Polarised training is one of the most-quoted ideas in endurance sport. It is also one of the most-misquoted. The shorthand "80/20" gets repeated on cycling forums every week, usually by riders who have never read the original work and who, if you watched their training files, are clearly not doing it.

Across multiple appearances on the Roadman Cycling Podcast, [Prof. Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler) has been unusually clear about what his model actually says, what it does not say, and where the amateur internet has it wrong. This page is the distillation. Five positions he holds consistently, with the practical application underneath each.

## Who Stephen Seiler is

Seiler is the American-born exercise physiologist based at the University of Agder in Norway. He came to the polarised model the long way round — by analysing how Norwegian rowers and cross-country skiers were already training, not by designing a study to test a hypothesis. That distinction matters and he raises it repeatedly. The model came from observation of what worked at the elite level. The science arrived afterward.

Full background sits on [Stephen Seiler's guest page](/guests/stephen-seiler).

## Position 1 — Most of your training has to be genuinely easy

This is the foundation. On his first long-form Roadman appearance, Seiler put it bluntly: the elite athletes he studied were not doing more hard sessions than recreational riders. They were doing more easy sessions. The 80% number is not arbitrary. It is the volume floor below which the high-end work cannot land.

His specific framing: "below the first lactate threshold." Conversational pace. The kind of effort where you finish a four-hour ride and could comfortably go again the next morning. If your easy ride leaves you slightly cooked the day after, it was not an easy ride.

**How to apply it:** Audit your last four weeks. Count the rides that were genuinely below LT1 (talk test, low cardiac drift, RPE 3–4) and the rides that drifted into the grey zone. Most amateurs find the ratio is closer to 50/50 than 80/20. Fixing that one number is the cheapest fitness intervention available.

## Position 2 — The model is descriptive, not prescriptive

This is the position Seiler has been clearest about across his more recent Roadman conversations, and it is the one most riders miss. The 80/20 split was observed in elite athletes training 25 to 35 hours a week. It was never proposed as a universal prescription for working amateurs.

On his fourth appearance — what we call the "6-hour problem" episode — Seiler addressed this directly. A rider with 6 hours of training a week who tries to allocate 80% of that to easy riding is left with a tiny intensity dose that may not be enough to push fitness forward. The structure works at scale. It struggles when total volume is small.

**How to apply it:** Use your weekly hours as the calibration variable, not the percentage. Above ~10 hours per week, polarised distribution makes mechanical sense. Below 6 hours, a pyramidal split — more sweet spot and threshold, less pure easy — is usually a better fit. The 6–10 hour range is the contested middle. See [polarised vs sweet spot training](/blog/polarised-vs-sweet-spot-training) for the working comparison.

## Position 3 — The grey zone is the real enemy

Seiler has used a version of the same line in three separate Roadman episodes: the worst training week is not the one that is too easy or too hard. It is the one that is uniformly moderate. The grey zone sits above LT1 but below LT2 — too hard to recover from, too easy to drive top-end adaptation.

His wording on the polarised training episode was that grey zone work is the "default mistake of self-coached athletes." Rides that feel productive because they are uncomfortable, but produce flat fitness because the body cannot recover between them and cannot fully express the high-end stimulus when it does try to go hard.

**How to apply it:** Look for the rides in your week where heart rate sits between 75% and 85% of max for sustained periods. That is the grey zone. Either slow them down to genuine easy, or shorten them and turn them into proper threshold work. The middle is where the watts go to die.

## Position 4 — Hard sessions are simpler than people think

When Seiler talks about the 20%, he is consistently boring about it on purpose. The hard sessions in his data are not exotic. They are 4×8-minute, 4×16-minute, or longer sub-threshold blocks at 90–95% of LT2. Repeated. For years.

In the 80/20 conversation he made the point that most elite intervals are not magic. They are the same simple sessions executed with high consistency and adequate recovery. Time at intensity matters more than peak watts. A clean 4×8 at 100% of FTP is a better session than a ragged 5×5 that drifts because the rider is fried from accumulated grey-zone fatigue.

**How to apply it:** Pick two interval sessions and run them for eight to twelve weeks. 4×8 at FTP is the canonical Seiler-style block. 4×16 at sweet spot is the longer-form version. Resist the urge to rotate sessions every week. Adaptation is what consistency buys you.

## Position 5 — Intensity discipline beats the ratio

The fifth position pulls the others together. Seiler's view, repeated across his most recent appearances, is that the precise distribution percentage matters less than the discipline to keep easy sessions easy and hard sessions hard. A rider whose easy days are genuinely easy and whose hard days are genuinely hard will improve on almost any reasonable distribution. A rider who cannot police intensity will not improve regardless of the model on paper.

This is the line that connects his sports-science position to the working coach's view. [Joe Friel](/guests/joe-friel) describes the same phenomenon in his own language. [Dan Lorang](/guests/dan-lorang) has said it in different words on his Roadman appearances. The label on the model — polarised, pyramidal, sweet spot — is a second-order question. The first-order question is: are you committing to the intensity you have decided on, or drifting between zones because no one is holding you to it?

**How to apply it:** Decide what each session is. Write the target zone at the top of the workout file. If a ride is meant to be easy, ride it easy even when you feel good. If a session is meant to be hard, fuel it properly and execute the prescribed intervals. The discipline is the work.

## Where he has updated his own framing

What makes Seiler unusual among researchers is how openly he revises his position in public. The original 80/20 framing was tightened across his Roadman episodes as he saw the model misapplied. He pushed back on coaches who were prescribing pure polarised distribution to amateur athletes. He acknowledged the role of sweet spot for time-crunched riders. He sharpened the talk-test and LT1/LT2 definitions.

If you only know the headline percentage, you have the 2010 version of his thinking. The current version is more nuanced and, frankly, more useful for the rider who is actually putting the work in.

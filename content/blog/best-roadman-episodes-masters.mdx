The "Not Done Yet" framing is not a tagline. It is the deepest emotional truth of the Roadman audience. Most of the people who listen to this podcast are between 35 and 55, with a job, a family, and the quiet refusal to accept that their best riding is behind them. The cycling internet has plenty to say to twenty-somethings. There is much less aimed at the rider who has fifteen years of training history, a gradually declining recovery capacity, and the mental clarity to want to do this properly.

These are the twelve episodes I would queue up for a masters cyclist starting from scratch with the Roadman archive. Curated, in order, with the takeaway from each.

## Start here

If you only listen to one episode, start with **[Why You're Slow After 40 and How to Fix It](/podcast/ep-2139-why-youre-slow-after-40-and-how-to-fix-it-rider-support)**. It is the cleanest entry point. We walk through the actual physiology — what genuinely declines with age, what does not, and what most riders confuse for ageing that is really just training and recovery debt accumulating.

The takeaway: most "I am slowing down because I am older" stories are training stories, not age stories. The training fix usually delivers more than the riders expect.

## The twelve episodes, in order

**1. [Why You're Slow After 40 and How to Fix It](/podcast/ep-2139-why-youre-slow-after-40-and-how-to-fix-it-rider-support)**
Guest: rider support format with Anthony.
Takeaway: separates real biological decline from accumulated training debt. Most amateur slowdown is the second, not the first, and is fixable inside a single training block.

**2. [The Hard Truth: Why Cyclists Over 40 Slow Down (and How to Beat It)](/podcast/ep-2200-hard-truth-why-cyclists-over-40-slow-down-how-to-beat-it-rdm)**
Guest: rider support format.
Takeaway: the second-tier decline conversation. Where strength loss, hormonal change, and recovery capacity actually shift, and the practical interventions that hold each one off.

**3. [New Study Finally Confirms What Winning Masters Cyclists Have Known](/podcast/ep-new-study-finally-confirms-what-winning-masters-cyclists-have-known)**
Guest: Anthony breaks down the research.
Takeaway: the scientific case that masters who train correctly hold performance for far longer than the standard age-decline curve suggests. The methods are not exotic — they are consistency and recovery.

**4. [How Cyclists Can Get Lean — and Stay Lean — Forever, with Alex Larson](/podcast/ep-2088-how-cyclists-can-get-lean-stay-lean-forever-alex-larson)**
Guest: Alex Larson, sports dietitian.
Takeaway: body composition for cyclists without crash dieting. Particularly relevant for masters because metabolic flexibility, not calorie restriction, is the lever. Eating to fuel performance is what produces sustainable leanness.

**5. [How Joe Friel Structures the Ideal Cycling Training Week](/podcast/ep-40-how-joe-friel-structures-the-ideal-cycling-training-week)**
Guest: [Joe Friel](/guests/joe-friel).
Takeaway: the canonical masters-friendly training week from the coach who literally wrote the book. Periodisation, deload weeks, and the discipline that separates the riders who keep improving from those who plateau.

**6. [80/20 Training to Ride Faster, with Dr Stephen Seiler](/podcast/ep-2148-80-20-training-to-ride-faster-dr-stephen-seiler)**
Guest: [Prof. Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler).
Takeaway: polarised training is disproportionately useful for masters because the recovery cost of grey-zone riding is higher with age. Genuinely easy easy days are not optional after 40.

**7. [Roglič's Coach Builds a Training Plan for Amateur Riders](/podcast/ep-2134-roglics-coach-builds-a-training-plan-for-amateur-riders-dan)**
Guest: [Dan Lorang](/guests/dan-lorang).
Takeaway: how a World Tour coach scales his pro methodology down to a working amateur week. The principles do not change with age — they are simply protected more carefully.

**8. [Strength Training for Cycling, Simplified, with Derek Teel](/podcast/ep-2183-strength-training-for-cycling-simplified-derek-teel)**
Guest: Derek Teel.
Takeaway: strength training is the single most important intervention for masters cyclists. Sarcopenia accelerates after 40 and is largely preventable. Two sessions a week of structured strength work is the floor, not the ceiling.

**9. [Cycling Fast at a Low Heart Rate, with Prof Seiler](/podcast/ep-2095-secret-to-cycling-fast-at-a-low-heart-rate-prof-seiler)**
Guest: Prof. Stephen Seiler.
Takeaway: how to train aerobic capacity at controlled intensities. Particularly useful for masters who have less margin for error on hard days and need to extract everything possible from easy ones.

**10. [How Pro Cyclists Boost Performance With This Magic Drink](/podcast/ep-2114-how-pro-cyclists-boost-their-performance-with-this-magic-drink)**
Guest: featured nutrition discussion.
Takeaway: in-ride fuelling for hard sessions. Masters cyclists are particularly likely to be under-fuelled on the bike and disproportionately likely to benefit from getting it right.

**11. [How to Improve Core Strength for Cyclists, Part 2](/podcast/ep-2250-how-to-improve-core-strength-for-cyclists-part-2)**
Guest: featured strength conversation.
Takeaway: core strength is the structural piece masters tend to undertrain. Saddle pain, low back issues, and end-of-ride power loss often trace back here.

**12. [How to Cycle Fast With a Low Heart Rate](/podcast/ep-2066-how-to-cycle-fast-with-a-low-heart-rate)**
Guest: rider-support format.
Takeaway: the closing piece on intensity discipline. The masters cyclists who keep improving are the ones who police easy days hardest.

## Why these twelve

Three patterns run through every episode in the list.

The first is that masters cyclists do not need different training. They need better-targeted training. The principles are identical to younger riders. The recovery cost of getting them wrong is higher.

The second is that strength training and sleep are the two interventions that pay disproportionately as you age. Both are typically undertrained. Both cost very little to add to a programme. Both compound over years.

The third is that body composition without restrictive dieting is non-negotiable. The masters who get faster are not the ones cutting calories. They are the ones eating to fuel performance and letting body composition follow. Alex Larson's episode covers this in the most depth.

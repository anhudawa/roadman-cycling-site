Dan Lorang is one of the few coaches in the world who has won at the top of cycling and the top of long-course triathlon at the same time. Three Ironman world titles with Jan Frodeno. A Kona course record with Anne Haug. Grand Tour stage wins as the head of performance at Red Bull-Bora-Hansgrohe. Now coaching Lucy Charles-Barclay and Taylor Knibb. He announced earlier this year that he will leave the team after the 2026 Tour de France, ending a ten-year spell.

He has been on the Roadman Cycling Podcast more often than any other working coach. This page is what he says about endurance specifically — the long aerobic system that sits underneath every other adaptation. Five positions, in his own framing, with how to apply each.

For his full background, see [Dan Lorang's guest page](/guests/dan-lorang).

## Position 1 — The aerobic base is the only ceiling that matters

Lorang has said a version of this on every Roadman appearance. The aerobic system — mitochondrial density, capillary network, fat oxidation, stroke volume — is the slow-built foundation that determines how much threshold and VO2 work the body can absorb later. Get the base wrong and everything you build on top of it has a low ceiling.

His framing on the Bora-Hansgrohe periodisation episode was that World Tour athletes spend most of the year building or protecting aerobic capacity. The "training" most amateurs picture — intervals, threshold work, race-pace efforts — is a small fraction of the year, and only really productive when sat on top of the right base.

**How to apply it:** Spend the first 8 to 12 weeks of any training cycle building base. Volume at low intensity is not optional. Skipping the base phase to "get to real training faster" is the most common periodisation mistake Lorang names on the podcast.

## Position 2 — The long ride is the non-negotiable session

The line Lorang has used on Roadman that gets quoted most often: if an athlete can only do two sessions in a week, one of them should always be the long aerobic ride. Not a hammer-fest. A controlled three-to-five-hour ride that builds the engine every other session depends on.

He has explained the logic across multiple episodes. The long ride drives adaptations that shorter sessions cannot replicate — fat oxidation under glycogen depletion, durability under accumulated load, the slow shift in fibre type that only happens with hours under tension. For triathletes preparing for Ironman, it is the structural backbone of the cycling block. For World Tour riders preparing for Grand Tours, the same logic applies.

**How to apply it:** Block out the long ride in the calendar before everything else. Three hours minimum for a serious amateur on most weeks, four to five when the schedule allows. Keep it controlled — Zone 2 by power, conversational by feel, with deliberate fuelling throughout.

## Position 3 — Hard sessions need to be properly fuelled

Lorang returns to fuelling in almost every Roadman appearance, and he is consistent about the numbers. His pros target 90 to 120 grams of carbohydrate per hour during hard sessions, using multiple transportable carbs to clear the absorption ceiling. Hard sessions in a glycogen-depleted state are not training adaptations. They are completing a workout while leaving most of the stimulus on the floor.

He is also clear that the figures do not transfer cleanly to amateurs without preparation. Gut training is required. Most age-group athletes ride hard sessions on 30 to 40g/hr — a third of what their pros consume — and then wonder why power drops in the final intervals. The session quality collapses, the adaptation signal weakens, and the rider walks away tired without the training response they were chasing.

**How to apply it:** Build fuelling for hard sessions deliberately. Start at 60g/hr and work up across multiple weeks until 90g/hr feels routine. Track it. The carbohydrate dose is part of the session, not a side note. Pair this with the Roadman [fuelling calculator](/tools/fuelling) for session-level planning.

## Position 4 — Strength training is part of endurance, not separate from it

Lorang programmes year-round strength work for his triathletes and his cycling riders. On the Roadman Frodeno episode he framed it directly: strength is not a discipline that competes with endurance. It is a tool that protects the body's ability to express endurance. Improved cycling economy means a given power output costs less metabolically. Stronger structural systems mean the rider can absorb more training load before breaking.

His protocol description on the podcast is consistent — two sessions per week of structured strength work, with heavy compound lifts (3–5 reps), single-leg variation for imbalance, and posterior chain emphasis. Not circuit-style cardio. Not 15-rep bodyweight metcons. Real load, with progressive overload, sequenced into the training week.

**How to apply it:** Two strength sessions a week. Time them so they do not compromise key bike sessions — most coaches programme strength immediately after a hard ride, on the same day, so the recovery cost lands together rather than splitting across days. See the [Roadman strength training course](https://www.roadmancycling.com) for the structured version.

## Position 5 — Recovery is the same discipline as training

Lorang's most underrated point on Roadman is that the athletes who improve fastest are not the ones who train hardest. They are the ones who recover with the same level of intent. Sleep, nutrition timing, stress management, training density — the recovery side of the ledger is where adaptation actually happens.

He has been particularly clear about training density. The cyclists in his programme do not stack ten hard weeks and then collapse. Every third or fourth week is a deliberate reduced-load week, with volume cut by 30–40% while intensity is maintained. That is when the body absorbs what the previous block built.

**How to apply it:** Plan in 3+1 or 4+1 cycles. Three to four progressive weeks, one deload. Schedule the deload before you need it, not after. Treat sleep as a training variable: track it for two weeks, see what your real average is, and protect the bottom of the range first.

## Where his methods deviate from pure polarised

It is worth noting where Lorang's view differs from a strict reading of [Stephen Seiler](/guests/stephen-seiler)'s polarised model. Lorang agrees with the broad shape — most training easy, hard sessions genuinely hard, no grey zone — but he programmes more sweet spot and threshold work for athletes who do not have the volume to make a purely polarised distribution effective. For the time-limited amateur, that distinction matters. See [polarised vs sweet spot training](/blog/polarised-vs-sweet-spot-training) for the working comparison.

The other deviation is around individualisation. Lorang has said repeatedly that the model on paper is a starting point. The real coaching work is in adjusting the prescription to the specific athlete — their training history, their recovery capacity, their event demands, their life context. The principles scale. The numbers do not.

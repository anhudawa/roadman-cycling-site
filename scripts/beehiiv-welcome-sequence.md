**The #1 mistake is training in the grey zone.**

Every ride is "kind of hard." Nothing is genuinely easy. Nothing is genuinely brutal. You go out, you ride at a pace that feels productive, you come home tired, and you do it again three days later.

Professor Stephen Seiler calls it the black hole of training intensity. You're working hard enough to build fatigue but not hard enough to build fitness. Your body adapts to mediocrity and stops responding.

Here's what it looks like in practice:

- Your "easy" rides average 0.70-0.75 IF instead of 0.55-0.65
- Your "hard" sessions never truly hurt because you're always carrying fatigue from yesterday's "easy" ride that wasn't easy
- Your FTP hasn't moved in 6-12 months despite consistent training
- You feel tired all the time but never feel fast

**The fix is counterintuitive: ride easier on easy days so you can ride harder on hard days.**

Your Zone 2 should feel embarrassingly easy. If you're not getting passed by people on city bikes, you're probably going too hard. I'm not joking.

And your hard days $€” the intervals, the threshold work, the VO2max efforts $€” should be genuinely painful. Not "a bit uncomfortable." The kind of hard where you're questioning your life choices in the last interval.

That polarisation $€” truly easy and truly hard, with very little in between $€” is what separates riders who improve from riders who plateau.

One thing to try this week: on your next easy ride, drop your power by 10-15% from what feels comfortable. Make it boring. Make it feel pointless. That's probably where it should have been all along.
